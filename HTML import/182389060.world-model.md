# A Reinforcement Learning View of the Human World

_This post assumes you have read Barto & Sutton_

Reinforcement Learning (RL) studies how an agent learns to act in a world by interacting with it, receiving feedback, and updating its behavior over time. The same abstraction can be used to model human decision-making—not as a literal description of neural mechanisms, but as a computational-level framework for understanding learning and choice.

An RL system consists of states, actions, rewards, a policy, a value function, and a model of the world.

## State (s): What the world is like right now

**Formal RL**

A state is a representation of the environment at a given moment, containing all information relevant for decision-making.

**Human analogy**

The state includes:

- The external world (physical surroundings, social context)
    
- Your body (energy, health, emotions)
    
- Other people (their moods, power, intentions, constraints)
    

Importantly, humans never access the true state—only a perceived state, filtered by attention, memory, and belief.

Two people can be in the same objective situation and still occupy different “states” internally.

## Action (a): How you intervene in the world

**Formal RL**

An action is any choice the agent makes that influences the next state.

**Human analogy**

Actions include:

- Physical movement
    
- Speech
    
- Silence
    
- Allocation of attention
    
- Compliance or resistance
    

Even inaction is an action—it constrains how the state evolves.

## Reward (r): The feedback signal

**Formal RL**

A reward is a scalar signal indicating how desirable an outcome is.

**Human analogy**

Rewards are implemented biologically through systems like:

- Dopaminergic signaling (e.g., ventral tegmental area)
    
- Emotional reinforcement (pleasure, relief, pride, shame)
    

The RL framework captures reward’s _functional role_, not its mechanism. Biological systems use multiple parallel pathways (dopamine, opioids, serotonin) and distributed patterns, not simple scalars. But the key insights remain: rewards are local and noisy, they don’t encode long-term wellbeing, and what feels rewarding is not necessarily what is optimal.

## Policy (π): How actions are chosen

**Formal RL**

A policy maps states to actions: π(a|s)

**Human analogy**

Your policy is your internal rulebook: “When I see this situation, I usually do that.”

Policies can be:

- Deliberate (explicit reasoning)
    
- Habitual (learned through repetition)
    
- Inherited (copied from parents, peers, culture)
    

Most human behavior is policy execution, not policy optimization.

## Value Function (V): Long-term evaluation

**Formal RL**

The value function estimates how good a state is in the long run: V(s)

**Human analogy**

This is your implicit judgment: “Is being in this situation good for my future self?”

Value functions encode:

- Patience vs impulsivity (discount factor)
    
- Risk tolerance
    
- Meaning and identity
    

Two people can agree on immediate rewards but disagree on value.

## Action–Value Function (Q): How good a specific choice is

**Formal RL**

In addition to the value of a state, RL uses an action–value function: Q(s,a)

This estimates how good it is to take a specific action _a_ in a specific state _s_, and then continue following the current policy.

While the value function asks: “How good is being here?”

The Q-function asks: “How good is doing this right now?”

**Human analogy**

This corresponds to your moment-to-moment sense of:

- “If I say this... what happens?”
    
- “If I take this risk... is it worth it?”
    

High Q(s,a) means:

- You expect this action to lead to good outcomes
    
- You feel able to act
    
- You experience confidence and initiative
    

Low Q(s,a) means:

- You expect punishment, rejection, or futility
    
- You hesitate, freeze, or avoid
    

This is why confidence is not just personality—it is a learned estimate that your actions work in this world.

Trauma, failure, or social rejection often collapse Q(s,a) even when V(s) remains high: you may still believe a good life exists, but no longer believe you can reach it.

## Model of the World: How consequences are imagined

**Formal RL**

A model predicts how actions change states: P(s’|s,a)

**Human analogy**

This is your internal simulator: “If I do this, what will likely happen?”

It includes beliefs about:

- Social causality
    
- Institutions
    
- Power
    
- Trust
    
- Norms
    

Goal-setting depends entirely on this model. You cannot pursue outcomes you cannot imagine.

## Exploration vs. Exploitation: The Eternal Tradeoff

**Formal RL**

An agent must choose: exploit (maximize reward from current knowledge) or explore (try new actions to discover better options).

**Human analogy**

Childhood is exploration-heavy: trying identities, testing boundaries. The cost of mistakes is low, the value of information is high.

Adulthood shifts toward exploitation: careers, relationships, routines settle in. This makes sense when you’ve found something that works and exploration costs have risen.

But exploitation can fail:

- **Premature convergence**: Settling before the model is rich enough
    
- **Regret**: Realizing unexplored paths held higher value
    
- **Rigidity**: Continuing to exploit when the environment has changed
    

Many midlife crises are delayed exploration—attempts to sample the state space prematurely abandoned.

**The exploration paradox**: You need it most when stuck, but being stuck reduces capacity to explore (low resources, low confidence, high risk). External interventions—therapy, travel, new relationships—force exploration when internal mechanisms fail.

## Policy Iteration: Improving How You Act

**Formal RL**

Policy iteration is a learning process with two alternating steps:

1. **Policy evaluation**: Estimate how good the current policy is, using the value function
    
2. **Policy improvement**: Modify the policy to choose better actions in the same states
    

This cycle repeats until the policy stabilizes.

**Human analogy**

For humans, policy iteration does not look like numerical optimization. It appears as:

- Noticing recurring outcomes (”This keeps ending the same way.”)
    
- Experiencing friction, regret, or constraint
    
- Questioning whether a habitual response still fits the situation
    
- Trying a different response in similar states
    

Policy evaluation in humans is qualitative and narrative, not scalar: “Does this way of acting keep expanding my options, or shrinking them?”

Policy improvement is usually local and cautious, not global:

- Small behavioral changes
    
- Context-specific adjustments
    
- Testing alternatives in low-risk situations
    

Many people execute stable policies for long periods without running this loop explicitly.

## Value Iteration: Deciding What Is Worth Wanting

**Formal RL**

Value iteration focuses on learning the value function directly:

1. Estimate the long-term value of states
    
2. Choose actions that move toward higher-value states
    

The policy emerges implicitly from value estimates.

**Human analogy**

In humans, value iteration corresponds to re-evaluating priorities, not actions:

- “Is this kind of life actually good for me?”
    
- “What tradeoffs am I implicitly accepting?”
    
- “What future am I optimizing for without realizing it?”
    

This process often occurs during:

- Life transitions
    
- Failure or exhaustion
    
- Exposure to new environments
    
- Loss of meaning or identity conflict
    

Unlike policy iteration, value iteration can trigger large changes:

- Career shifts
    
- Relationship changes
    
- Redefinition of success
    
- Altered risk tolerance
    

When values shift, policies often follow automatically.

## Policy Iteration vs. Value Iteration in Humans

- **Policy iteration** changes how you act within a stable value system
    
- **Value iteration** changes what outcomes you consider worth pursuing
    

Most people:

- Perform limited policy iteration
    
- Rarely perform value iteration
    
- Confuse short-term reward signals with long-term value updates
    

Value iteration is rarer because it destabilizes identity, not just behavior.

## The Computational Intractability Problem

Human state spaces are astronomically large—formal RL algorithms are computationally impossible for biological systems.

**What humans actually do instead:**

Humans use approximations: narratives compress infinite states into manageable identities (”I’m the kind of person who...”), heuristics provide cheap rules (”when in doubt, be polite”), social learning outsources exploration to the collective, and emotion serves as cached value judgments refined over evolutionary time.

This means human “value iteration” is narrative reconstruction, not systematic search. “Policy improvement” is local adjustment, not global optimization. Most learning is model-free habit formation because model-based planning is too expensive.

The RL framework remains useful not because humans implement these algorithms, but because it describes the _computational problem_ humans solve with approximate, heuristic methods.

We are not RL agents. We are scrappy improvisers in an RL-shaped universe.

## Where the World Model Fits

Both policy and value iteration depend on the world model.

- If the model is wrong, policy improvement fails
    
- If the model is incomplete, some values never appear reachable
    
- If the model is rigid, iteration stops altogether
    

The world model defines the search space. Iteration only explores what the model allows to exist.

## Human Traits as Reinforcement Learning System Properties

**Goals** — “What I want” — High-value regions of state space under the current value function

**Motivation** — “How driven I feel” — Strength and reliability of reward prediction error (dopamine)

**Willpower** — “Self-control” — Ability to allocate compute, attention, and action budget to long-horizon policies

**Discipline** — “Being consistent” — Low policy entropy: choosing the same action in the same state

**Impulse control** — “Resisting temptation” — High discount factor (γ), where future rewards dominate immediate ones

**Resilience** — “Bouncing back” — Stability of the value function under negative reward shocks

**Confidence** — “Belief in myself” — High estimated Q(s,a) for actions involving agency and risk

**Anxiety** — “Worry” — A world model that predicts high probability of negative transitions

**Hope** — “Things can get better” — Belief that higher-value states are reachable

**Helplessness** — “Nothing I do matters” — Low perceived state–action controllability

**Meaning** — “My life makes sense” — Coherence between values, policy, and world model

**Identity** — “Who I am” — A compressed prior over which policies are allowed

**Trauma** — “Something broke in me” — A large negative reward that distorts the world model toward danger

**Habits** — “Automatic behavior” — Cached policy fragments learned through repetition

**Addiction** — “I can’t stop” — Reward function hijacked by a narrow, high-variance signal

**Curiosity** — “Wanting to explore” — Internal reward for reducing model uncertainty

**Creativity** — “New ideas” — Sampling out-of-distribution actions

**Growth** — “Becoming better” — Expansion of reachable high-value state space

**Wisdom** — “Knowing what matters” — A value function refined through sufficient exploration and validated against long-term outcomes

**Regret** — “I should have explored more” — Retrospective recognition that exploitation locked in suboptimal policies
