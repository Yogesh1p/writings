# Influencing Feelings

_Part of an ongoing series on the mechanics of feeling._

---

In the last few pieces, we talked about how feelings get compressed into words, how they travel across the gap between minds, and how stories manufacture what feels like value. All of that was about the internal mechanics — your feelings, approximated and transmitted.

This one is about something more uncomfortable.

What happens when someone else reaches in and moves the dial.

---

## Power is the ability to generate emotional response

Here is the cleanest definition of power I have arrived at: **power is the ability to generate an emotional response in someone and, through that, capture some portion of their agency.**

Not status. Not money. Not authority on paper.

The ability to make someone feel something — and have that feeling determine what they do next.

Every other kind of power is downstream of this. The executive who commands a room, the partner who knows exactly when to go quiet — what they share is the same thing: reliable access to your emotional controls. They know which knobs to turn. And crucially, they know that turning those knobs works.

This is why power is not distributed the way we think it is. It does not sit neatly at the top of org charts or income brackets. The employee with system access has power. The partner who withholds presence has power. Everyone, in every relationship they inhabit, holds some version of these controls over the people involved.

And the tools to generate emotional response at scale are available to anyone who learns to use them. Trump did not win the presidency because he outcompeted on policy expertise or institutional credibility. He won because he had more reliable access to a specific emotional frequency — grievance, pride, the feeling of being seen as the people who actually built something — than anyone else in the room, and he knew how to keep hitting it. The movement was not the vehicle. It was the proof of concept. Position followed feeling; it always does.

Money and status can buy proximity to people. They do not, by themselves, produce the feeling that makes someone hand you their agency. That requires something else — knowing what people are carrying underneath the surface, and naming it before they have found the words themselves.

The question is never _who has power_. It is _who knows they have it_ — and what they choose to do with it.

## The two levers

At the mechanical level, there are only two instruments.

You take away what someone values. Or you give them more of it.

Countries do this — sanctions, aid, trade. Organizations do this — bonuses, terminations. Relationships do this — presence and absence, warmth and withdrawal. The architecture is identical across all scales. Pain and reward are the controls. They mirror exactly how the mind manages the body: you generate discomfort to redirect attention, pleasure to reinforce a direction.

What makes this worth examining is not its simplicity but its ubiquity. You are using these levers constantly, whether you are conscious of it or not. The question is not whether you use them. It is whether you know you are.

## The first borrowed model

Most people’s first experience of love also happens to be their first experience of complete leverage asymmetry. The parent holds everything — safety, warmth, approval, survival itself. The child holds nothing equivalent in return. The love is real. The power gap is also real. Both things are true at once.

What that early dynamic does is install a model. Not inevitably, not uniformly — people carry it differently. But the role of the one who gives and the one who receives, the one who can withdraw and the one who cannot, gets learned early and at a formative depth.

Some people come out of it reaching for the role they did not have — the one with more leverage, more control over whether love gets given or withheld. In adult relationships, they move to ensure the other person has more to lose. They construct asymmetry, sometimes deliberately, sometimes without knowing it, because asymmetry feels like safety. The other person cannot leave as easily. They cannot take the love away.

It works in a narrow sense. The person with less leverage bends. They accommodate. They stay.

But what you have built is not intimacy. It is a management structure. And the part of the other person that notices this — even if they cannot name it — is slowly deciding something about you.

The borrowed model runs until you look at it directly. Most people never do.

## The machine problem

Imagine you are with someone and you know, with high confidence, how they will respond to your actions. You have mapped their emotional terrain. You know which input produces which output. You are not surprised anymore.

At some point, if you are honest with yourself, something starts to feel hollow.

Because you have reduced a person to a deterministic system. You are not relating to them. You are operating them. And there is a specific line — not always visible until you have crossed it — where the other person stops being a person to you and becomes more like a sophisticated interface. You push buttons. You get responses.

Humans are not deterministic. They make choices. They evolve. They carry contradiction. They have an interior life you cannot fully map. That irreducible unpredictability is not a flaw to be engineered away. It is the thing that makes them worth being with. The stochasticity is the point.

When you reduce it far enough, you have not won the relationship. You have ended it from the inside. You are just the last one to notice.

This is the deepest problem with the power game in relationships — not that it harms the other person, though it does. It destroys the thing you were trying to have.

## Stories are feeling factories — and everyone is selling

Music, film, poetry, advertising, political movements. Every one of these is, at its operating layer, a technology for manufacturing feeling in other people.

The songwriter does not sell you notes. They sell you the specific ache of a memory. The brand does not sell you a product. They sell you the feeling of being the kind of person who uses that product. Movements do not win people with policy arguments. They sell a feeling — of injury, of destiny, of enemies named and belonging confirmed — and people make enormous decisions from inside that emotional state.

This is the mechanism. Feelings are the substrate of decision-making. Stories are the most efficient known technology for producing feelings at scale. Influence, at its most sophisticated, is the ability to write the story that other people run their decisions through.

The uncomfortable implication: you are always running inside someone’s story. The only question is whether you chose it.

## The nonchalant problem

There is a specific person who makes this whole framework difficult: the one who is genuinely hard to move.

Most people experience this as a power imbalance by default. The detached person has more leverage because they are harder to destabilize. The more emotionally present person over-invests, bids up their emotional stakes, trying to get a response.

What is actually happening is a synchronization failure. The detached person is not powerful — they are often just disconnected. From themselves, from the relationship, from the feedback loop that makes mutual influence possible. You cannot genuinely influence someone whose receiver is off.

The mistake is reading this as a signal that you need to become more nonchalant yourself. The race to detachment ends somewhere neither person is actually present, and whatever the relationship was has become two people performing indifference at each other.

The real question in that dynamic is not _how do I gain the upper hand._ It is _what is generating the disconnect_ — and whether what you have is worth the diagnosis.

## The ethics is not where most people think it is

When people talk about the ethics of influence, they usually land on intent. _Did you mean well?_ That’s the wrong frame. Intent is invisible. It is also frequently wrong about itself — people with genuinely harmful patterns usually believe, with full sincerity, that they are operating from love.

The more useful frame is directional: does your influence leave the other person with more access to themselves, or less?

Some people, after sustained contact with you, become more capable of their own choices. They think more clearly. They know themselves better. They act from a more grounded place. Others, after sustained contact, become more confused, more dependent, less sure of their own perception.

You already know which dynamic you produce in people. You have seen the evidence across multiple relationships. The question is whether you have looked at it honestly.

The true test of character is not how someone behaves without power. It is what they do with it when they have it. When the leverage is in their favor. When they could. When no one would know.

What you do then is the actual answer. Not the answer you would give if asked. The answer in the room.

## What you are left with

Think about the people who have spent sustained time in your orbit. Not what they said about you — what they became. Did they get more themselves over time, or less? More capable of their own judgment, or more reliant on yours? More grounded, or more destabilized?

Most people have never asked this question directly. Not because the evidence is unavailable — it is sitting in the last five relationships — but because the answer carries a cost. Accepting that you have power over people means accepting responsibility for how you have used it. And most of us would rather believe we were simply loving, or simply struggling, than look at the specific shape of what we actually produced.

The true test of character is not how someone behaves without power. It is what they do with it when they have it — when the leverage is in their favor, when they could, when no one would know.

What you do then is the actual answer. Not the answer you would give if asked. The answer in the room.

_Next: Understanding Feelings — on reading what is actually happening beneath the surface, before the story gets written over it._

There is a version of persuasion that most people practice. It goes like this: you present your case clearly, you make yourself attractive, you list the reasons someone should say yes — and then you wait. Politely. Hopefully.

It rarely works. Not because the logic was wrong, but because logic was never the mechanism. People do not make decisions with their minds. They make decisions with their bodies — with tension and relief, with fear and desire — and then use their minds to write the story afterward.

Once you understand that, two things change. How you close a deal. And how you pursue a person.

---

## Part I: The Negotiation Table

### You are not presenting. You are reweighting.

The person sitting across from you at a negotiation already knows the stakes. They knew them before they walked in. They know what a failed deal means for their career, their credibility, the face they have to show their boss on Monday morning.

You did not come to inform them of this. You came to make it feel more real.

This is the difference between an amateur and a closer. The amateur adds information. The closer adjusts weight — they take facts the other party already holds and shift how heavily those facts land in the chest.

When you say _“you can take this offer, or go back home without a deal”_ — you have not told them anything new. But you have just made them picture themselves walking through their front door with nothing. The meeting room. The flight back. The conversation with whoever is waiting. That image, once vivid, pulls on them. You didn’t manufacture the fear. You crystallized it.

### The language mirror

Here is a more precise version of the same tool. If you know the person across from you answers to someone — a board, a boss, a partner — and you know how that someone speaks, use their words.

Not obviously. Not as mimicry. But if their boss talks about _“coming home with something solid”_, or _“not leaving value on the table”_, or _“closing strong”_ — and you use that phrase in the room, something happens beneath the surface. The other person hears their internal critic speaking. The weight shifts again.

This works because decisions that carry accountability are not just made in the room. They are made in anticipation of the conversation that follows. You are simply inviting that conversation into the present moment, where you can shape it.

### The ultimatum: closing window

_Take it or I walk._

Four words that can end a negotiation in your favor — or destroy your position entirely. Most people use this too early, too often, or without the willingness to follow through. All three are fatal.

The rule is simple: do not offer an ultimatum unless you are fully prepared to walk. Not 70% prepared. Not “I’ll walk if they call my bluff.” Fully. Because the moment you say it and then stay, you have just demonstrated that your hard limit isn’t real. Every position you hold for the rest of the conversation is now suspect.

Used correctly — at the end, with genuine resolve, when the deal is nearly there but the other side keeps testing the edges — it works because it removes the optionality they were banking on. You are not threatening them. You are informing them that the window is closing. Those are different things. One creates resistance. The other creates urgency.

### What closing actually looks like

A real close is not a dramatic moment. It’s not a line or a maneuver. It’s the natural end of a process in which you’ve made the cost of not deciding higher than the cost of deciding. You’ve made the alternative vivid. You’ve mirrored what they care about. You’ve held your position without flinching.

By the time you say the words, the decision is already made. You are just narrating the conclusion.

## Part II: The Romantic Arena

### The same mechanism, different ethics

Here is where most people go wrong when they try to apply deal-closing logic to dating: they treat attraction like a negotiation. They list their value. They make their case. They wait.

It doesn’t work — not because the logic is flawed, but because seduction is not a negotiation. There is no table. There is no term sheet. And the moment someone feels like they’re being closed, the spell breaks.

But the underlying psychology is not so different. People still decide with their feelings first. The difference is what kind of feelings you’re working with — and what kind of person you need to appear to be.

### The problem with rational value

If you have money, status, ambition, or exceptional looks — you can get someone’s attention. You might even get a yes. But here is the structural problem: a yes that is based entirely on what you have is only as durable as your position relative to the next person who has more.

Rational value gets you to the table. It does not keep you at it.

What keeps someone at the table — what makes them hard to replace — is something much harder to quantify: how you make them feel. Specifically, three things. Whether they feel _seen_. Whether they feel _understood_. Whether being around you makes them feel more like themselves, or more like a better version of themselves.

These are things that cannot be outbid. You cannot post a higher offer on “the person who finally gets me.”

### How to hold yourself

The most magnetic posture in pursuit — whether in a boardroom or a bar — is someone who wants without needing. Interest without desperation. Warmth without pressure.

This is not a performance. If it is, it collapses the moment there’s real tension. The goal is to genuinely arrive at a place where you are interested, but fundamentally okay either way. That internal state — not the affectation of it — is what reads as confidence.

The other thing that matters: appear harmless. Not passive. Not weak. But non-threatening. Charm requires safety. No one opens up to someone they are bracing against. Charisma is warmth plus ease plus the signal that this person will not use what you give them against you.

Push and neediness are the two ways this goes wrong. Push reads as desperation wearing aggression’s clothes. Neediness is just desperation, plainly dressed. Both make the other person contract.

### One rule you don’t carry over from deals

The emotional reweighting technique — making the cost of walking away feel vivid and heavy — has no place here.

In a negotiation, both parties accept they are in a game with pressure. The rules include it. In romance, manufacturing dread to force a decision is not closing. It is coercion with softer language. If someone chooses you because you made the alternative feel unbearable, you have not won their desire. You have manufactured compliance. It will cost you later, and it should.

Attraction that is built correctly does not need a closing line. The person chooses because they want to. Your job is to make wanting to feel natural — not to make not wanting to feel impossible.

## Part III: Every Relationship Is a Different Game

People speak about relationships as if they are all the same structure with different faces in the seats. They are not. Each one has a different architecture, a different primary currency, a different load-bearing wall. Misread which one you are in, and you will keep doing the right things for the wrong game.

**The playfulness bond** is held together by wit, lightness, mutual teasing. Seriousness is the enemy here — not because depth is bad, but because the container of this particular relationship was built for play. Bring gravity in and the whole thing stiffens.

**The deep understanding bond** is built on being truly known — which requires vulnerability, patience, and the willingness to keep paying attention over time. It is the most durable architecture when built properly. It is also the slowest to construct.

**The passion bond** runs hot. High intensity, high aliveness, high stakes. It is not sustainable without active renewal — you cannot coast inside it. What felt electric at the start becomes ordinary if neither person keeps choosing to bring heat.

**The provider bond** is built on security and care — one person, or both, finding love expressed through protection, provision, stability. It erodes when that function disappears or goes unacknowledged. Appreciation is the maintenance.

**The ambition bond** is two people who sharpen each other, grow together, use the relationship as fuel rather than shelter. Shared direction is the glue. The danger is when one person’s trajectory diverges and the other cannot follow.

**The comfort bond** is a refuge. Predictability, ease, and peace are the love language. This is not the absence of passion — it is a different flavor of devotion. It is underrated, particularly by people who mistake stimulation for depth.

**The mirror bond** deserves its own warning. This is the relationship built on one person reflecting an idealized image back to the other — or both doing it for each other. It feels like being understood, but it is actually being affirmed. The distinction matters enormously. Genuine understanding survives you changing. A mirror bond does not. The moment one person grows out of the image, the whole structure becomes unstable.

Most real relationships contain two or three of these layered together. The art is knowing which one is primary — because that is the one you have to protect.

---

## The Unifying Principle

Whether you are closing a deal or pursuing a person, the same thing is true at the bottom of it all: you are not managing information. You are managing feeling.

The closer who wins is not the one with the best terms on paper. It is the one who made the cost of not deciding feel real. The person who wins in pursuit is not the one with the best list of attributes. It is the one who made being with them feel like a specific thing that cannot be found anywhere else.

Logic gets you in the room. Feeling gets you to yes.

The only difference between the boardroom and the first date is what kind of yes you are after — and therefore what kind of pressure, if any, is ethical to apply.

Know which room you are in. Play accordingly.
