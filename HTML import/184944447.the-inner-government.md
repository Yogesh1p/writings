Most people think of the self as a single voice.

It isn’t.

It’s closer to a country—full of factions, impulses, long-term interests, foreign pressures, and moments of crisis. When life goes wrong, it’s rarely because one part is evil. It’s because the wrong part seized power.

What follows is a model I use to think about the inner life—not as a personality test, not as mysticism, but as statecraft.

### Why Think in Terms of Government?

Governments exist for one reason: to manage competing interests over time without collapse.

Your inner life has desires that want satisfaction now, thoughts that want coherence, values that want continuity, and a world that constantly interferes. Ignoring this plurality doesn’t make it go away. It just ensures that decisions are made by coups instead of consent.

### The Cabinet Members

To govern, you must know who is sitting at the table.

1. The Empire (Continuity Over Time)

The Empire is not a voice. It represents who you are becoming across decades. It does not argue, and it does not panic.

- **Role:** Preserve core values and maintain identity across change.
    
- **Power:** Absolute veto over actions that violate foundational principles.
    
- **Limitation:** It cannot react quickly to daily events.
    

2. The Thinker (Strategy and Truth)

The Thinker is the architect. It analyzes, models, predicts, and names tradeoffs.

- **Role:** Long-term planning, truth-seeking, and coherence.
    
- **Power:** Proposes strategies and calls for pauses.
    
- **Limitation:** It cannot rule. When the Thinker dominates, life freezes into endless analysis.
    

3. The Foreign Minister (Reality Interface)

The Foreign Minister deals with the outside world: other people, social feedback, risk, and consequences.

- **Role:** Interpret reality, negotiate boundaries, and adjust tactics.
    
- **Power:** De-escalates conflict and translates external signals.
    
- **Limitation:** It must not govern identity. When overactive, it leads to people-pleasing.
    

4. The People (Energy, Emotion, Meaning)

The People are plural—the dreamer, the worker, the child, the romantic. They are not irrational; they are informational.

- **Role:** Provide motivation and signal unmet needs.
    
- **Power:** They generate the energy required to act.
    
- **Limitation:** They cannot see long-term consequences.
    

5. The Executive (Action)

The Executive is the hand that signs the paper. It forms habits and executes choices. It acts on the will of the government.

### The Council Protocol: How High-Priority Decisions Are Made

Most internal suffering comes from a disorderly process. The People scream, the Thinker panics, and the Executive acts impulsively to stop the noise.

For low-stakes decisions, habit suffices.

But for high-priority decisions—career pivots, relationship commitments, ethical dilemmas—you must assemble the Council.

You cannot let the loudest voice win. You must let every voice speak, then let the Empire decide.

Here is the protocol.

#### Phase 1: The Hearing

You pause the Executive. No action is taken yet. You invite the factions to the table and demand they speak clearly.

First, The People speak.

Query: “What do we feel? What is the energy demanding?”

The Voice: “We are exhausted. We feel dead in this current situation. We crave adventure, even if it’s dangerous. If we stay here, we will rebel.”

Second, The Foreign Minister reports.

Query: “What is the terrain? What are the risks?”

The Voice: “The market is volatile. Our reputation is currently secure, but a pivot creates social friction. However, staying put risks obsolescence. The external world is signaling change.”

Third, The Thinker analyzes.

Query: “Is there a viable path? Do the numbers work?”

The Voice: “The risk is statistically high. We have six months of runway. However, the cost of inaction is a guaranteed decline in mental acuity. A pivot is illogical in the short term, but necessary for the long-term algorithm.”

#### Phase 2: The Deliberation

At this stage, there is conflict. The Foreign Minister is worried about safety; The People are screaming for freedom; The Thinker is paralyzed by the data.

Usually, this is where we dissociate or doom-scroll.

Instead, you turn to the head of the table.

#### Phase 3: The Imperial Decree

When the cabinet is deadlocked, the Empire breaks the tie. The Empire does not care about comfort (The People), safety (Foreign Minister), or perfect logic (The Thinker). It cares about the Constitution of the Self.

The Empire Speaks:

“The Foreign Minister is right that the danger is real.

The Thinker is right that the plan is imperfect.

But our Constitution states that we value autonomy over comfort.

To stay is to violate who we are becoming.

Therefore, the risk is accepted.”

#### Phase 4: Executive Action

Once the Empire speaks, the debate ends.

The Executive is given a clear mandate: Resign and pivot.

Because the decision came from the Empire, the other factions fall in line. The Thinker stops panicking and starts planning the budget. The Foreign Minister stops worrying about approval and starts managing the exit strategy. The People provide the energy to push through.

### Conclusion: Governing vs. Drifting

You cannot eliminate the conflict between your head, your heart, and the world. That conflict is the engine of human consciousness.

But you can stop the coups.

The next time you face a crisis (too many voices), do not ask “What should I do?”

Ask: “Has the Council met?”

Let them argue. Let them present their evidence.

Then, let the Empire rule.
