### The Transformer: The Problem with Sequential Thinking

Before the Transformer existed, sequence modeling meant recurrence. An RNN processes tokens one at a time, left to right, threading a hidden state forward. To understand token 500, the model must have correctly compressed all information from tokens 1 through 499 into a fixed-size vector. This is a bottleneck that is not merely computational — it is _structural_. The past is filtered through a narrow corridor, and distant information decays or vanishes.

LSTMs helped with gating mechanisms, but the problem remained: you cannot parallelize a recurrence. Training on long sequences was slow, and long-range dependencies were unreliable. The field needed a different inductive bias.

Vaswani et al. (2017) asked a simple question: what if every token could directly talk to every other token, simultaneously, without any sequential bottleneck? The answer was the attention mechanism formalized into a full architecture.

For a sequence of tokens, each token is projected into three vectors: a query $\mathbf{q}$, a key $\mathbf{k}$, and a value $\mathbf{v}$. The output for each token is a weighted sum of all value vectors, where the weight between token $i$ and token $j$ is determined by the compatibility of $\mathbf{q}_i$ and $\mathbf{k}_j$:

The $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling prevents the dot products from growing so large that the softmax saturates into near-zero gradients. To give the model multiple “perspectives” on the same sequence, multiple attention heads are run in parallel and their outputs concatenated:

$$\text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h),\mathbf{W}^O$$

$$\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_i^Q,; \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_i^K,; \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}_i^V)$$

Between attention layers, a position-wise feed-forward network processes each token independently:

$$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = \max(0,; \mathbf{x}\mathbf{W}_1 + \mathbf{b}_1),\mathbf{W}_2 + \mathbf{b}_2$$

Because attention has no notion of sequence order, position is injected via sinusoidal embeddings added to the input token embeddings. Each position $pos$ and dimension $i$ gets a fixed signal:

$$PE_{(pos,, 2i)} = \sin!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \qquad PE_{(pos,, 2i+1)} = \cos!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right)$$

The Transformer was introduced as a translation model, but its implications were immediately broader. Every token attends to every other token in a single pass. Training is fully parallelizable. Long-range dependencies cost no more than short-range ones. The architecture was, in retrospect, not a model for translation — it was a general-purpose reasoning substrate waiting to be scaled.

What it unlocked: parallel training on GPUs at unprecedented scale.

What it left open: the Transformer was still an encoder-decoder architecture, trained on paired translation data. Nobody had yet asked what happens when you take just the decoder side and train it on raw text to predict the next token.

### GPT-1: The Pretraining Hypothesis

The Transformer showed that attention could replace recurrence. But it was trained on a supervised task — translate this sentence. The question Radford et al. (2018) at OpenAI asked was different: what if we trained a large Transformer decoder on a massive corpus of text, predicting each next token given all previous tokens? No labels, no pairs — just raw language.

The pretraining objective is simple. Given a sequence of tokens $\mathcal{U} = {u_1, \ldots, u_n}$, maximize the log-likelihood:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{i} \log P(u_i \mid u_{i-k}, \ldots, u_{i-1};, \Theta)$$

where $k$ is the context window and $\Theta$ are the model parameters. After pretraining, the model is fine-tuned on downstream labeled tasks — classification, entailment, similarity — with a lightweight task-specific head on top of the frozen representations.

GPT-1 had 117 million parameters and a context window of 512 tokens. It showed, for the first time, that a single pretrained model could be fine-tuned to competitive performance on a wide range of NLP benchmarks — commonsense reasoning, textual entailment, question answering. The representations learned by predicting text were rich enough to transfer.

The architectural choices were clean: 12 layers of decoder-only Transformer blocks, causal (left-to-right) attention masks so the model cannot see future tokens, and learned positional embeddings replacing the sinusoidal ones. Layer normalization was placed at the beginning of each sub-block (pre-norm rather than post-norm), a choice that would prove more stable at scale.

What GPT-1 proved: pretraining on raw text is not a hack. It is a principled way to learn about language before being told the task. The representations are transferable.

What it left as a question: how much does scale matter? GPT-1 was 117M parameters trained on the BooksCorpus dataset — large for 2018, but modest by any subsequent standard. Nobody knew yet whether the quality of these representations would continue improving with larger models and more data, or whether there was a ceiling.

### GPT-2: Scale as a Research Statement

Radford et al. (2019) did not change the architecture. They scaled it — to 1.5 billion parameters, trained on a new web-scale dataset called WebText (40GB of curated Reddit-linked text). The context window grew to 1,024 tokens.

The architectural changes were minimal but principled. Layer normalization moved to the input of each sub-block rather than the output. An additional layer normalization was added after the final self-attention block. The weight initialization was scaled by $1/\sqrt{N}$ where $N$ is the number of residual layers, to keep activation magnitudes stable at depth.

The result was striking and, at the time, controversial: GPT-2 could generate coherent multi-paragraph text. It achieved state-of-the-art on 7 of 8 language modeling benchmarks zero-shot — without any fine-tuning, simply by conditioning on a prompt. On the Penn Treebank, for instance, the perplexity without any task-specific training was lower than many purpose-built models of the era.

The deeper claim of GPT-2 was a hypothesis about task framing. A language model trained on enough diverse text implicitly learns to do many tasks — because the web contains examples of question-answering, translation, summarization, and reasoning in natural text form. A model good enough at next-token prediction over this distribution must, to some extent, have learned to do all of them. This would later be called the “task-agnostic” paradigm, and it set the intellectual foundation for everything that followed.

What GPT-2 demonstrated: scale matters, and the returns had not yet plateaued. The best model of the era on raw text was not a carefully engineered specialist but a large general predictor.

What it left open: the context window was still 1,024 tokens. The model could not coherently process a long document. Attention to tokens near the beginning of a 1,024-token window was already weaker than to recent tokens. Something was needed to let the model remember further back without expanding the quadratic cost of full attention.

### Transformer-XL: Breaking the Context Barrier with Memory

Here is the concrete problem. A Transformer decoder processes sequences in fixed-length segments. During training, a document longer than the context window is split into chunks: chunk 1 is tokens 1–512, chunk 2 is tokens 513–1024, and so on. Each chunk is processed independently. There is no mechanism for chunk 2 to know anything about chunk 1. A sentence straddling a chunk boundary is severed — its beginning is invisible to the attention of its end.

At inference, this creates the “context fragmentation” problem. When generating the first token of chunk 2, the model has no access to chunk 1 at all. Every chunk boundary resets the model’s memory to zero.

Dai et al. (2019) noticed that you cannot solve this by simply making the context window larger — cost scales as $O(n^2)$, so doubling the window quadruples training time. The solution had to reuse computation already done for previous segments without recomputing it.

The idea: cache the hidden states from the previous segment and allow the current segment to attend to them — but treat those cached states as fixed, not as part of the current computation graph. This is the **recurrence mechanism**.

Formally, let $\mathbf{H}_\tau^n$ denote the hidden states at layer $n$ for segment $\tau$. When processing segment $\tau + 1$, the keys and values at each layer are computed from the concatenation of cached and new states:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{\tau+1}^{n-1} = \left[\text{SG}(\mathbf{H}_\tau^{n-1}); \circ; \mathbf{H}_{\tau+1}^{n-1}\right]$$

$$\mathbf{k}_{\tau+1}^n = \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{\tau+1}^{n-1} \mathbf{W}_k^n, \qquad \mathbf{v}_{\tau+1}^n = \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{\tau+1}^{n-1} \mathbf{W}_v^n$$

$$\mathbf{q}_{\tau+1}^n = \mathbf{H}_{\tau+1}^{n-1} \mathbf{W}_q^n$$

The notation $\text{SG}(\cdot)$ means stop-gradient — the cached states are used for attention but do not participate in backpropagation. This means the recurrence does not create vanishing gradient problems across segments.

Because the model now attends to tokens from a previous segment, the positional encoding scheme must change. You cannot use absolute positions — the same position index would be used for tokens in both the cached segment and the current one. Transformer-XL introduced **relative positional encodings**: instead of encoding absolute position, encode the relative distance $r = i - j$ between query $i$ and key $j$. The attention score between position $i$ and position $j$ becomes:

$$A_{i,j} = \mathbf{q}_i^\top \mathbf{W}_Q^\top \mathbf{W}_K \mathbf{k}_j + \mathbf{q}_i^\top \mathbf{W}Q^\top \mathbf{W}{K,R} \mathbf{r}_{i-j} + u^\top \mathbf{W}_K \mathbf{k}j + v^\top \mathbf{W}{K,R} \mathbf{r}_{i-j}$$

where $\mathbf{r}_{i-j}$ is a sinusoidal encoding of the relative distance, and $u$, $v$ are learned global bias terms. This decomposition separates content-based and position-based attention into four additive terms.

The effective context length in Transformer-XL is not just the current segment size $L$ but $L \times N_{\text{layers}}$ in the best case — because information can propagate through the cache over multiple layers. Empirically on enwiki8 (character-level language modeling), Transformer-XL captured dependencies 80% longer than a standard Transformer and was 1800× faster than a comparable vanilla Transformer at evaluation time due to segment-level caching.

What Transformer-XL proved: memory and recurrence can coexist with attention. Context does not need to be a fixed, hard window.

What it left unresolved: the recurrence mechanism is sequential at inference — you must process segments in order, and the effective context is bounded by the number of cached layers times the segment size. For very long documents — 10,000 or 100,000 tokens — you still cannot bring all the content into a single attention pass. A more radical rethinking of which pairs of tokens actually need to attend to each other was required.

### Longformer: Structured Sparsity at Document Scale

The quadratic cost of full attention, $O(n^2)$, means that processing a 4,096-token document costs 64× more than a 512-token one. Most document-level tasks — scientific paper classification, legal contract review, entire-book question answering — require sequences in this regime. GPT-2’s 1,024-token limit simply excluded them.

But here is the empirical observation that makes progress possible: in a long document, most attention weights are negligible. A token discussing a method in the abstract rarely needs to strongly attend to a table number deep in the appendix. The attention matrix is sparse in practice — many entries are near zero — even though the algorithm computes all of them.

Beltagy, Peters, and Cohan (2020) asked: what if we built a model that only computed the attention entries that will actually be large? The challenge is predicting which entries those are before computing them. Their answer was to use a fixed structural prior, combining two patterns:

The first pattern is local window attention. Each token attends to a symmetric window of $w/2$ tokens on each side. This captures local syntactic context — the kind of information that dominates sentence-level understanding.

The second pattern is global attention. A small set of task-specific tokens — the `[CLS]` token in classification, the question tokens in question answering — attend to the entire sequence and are attended to by the entire sequence. This provides a pathway for distant information to flow: a token anywhere in the document can communicate with the global tokens, and through them influence tokens anywhere else.

The resulting attention pattern per token is:

$$a_{ij} = \begin{cases} \text{full softmax attention} & \text{if } i \text{ or } j \text{ is a global token} \ \text{local attention, window } w & \text{if } |i - j| \leq w/2 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The total number of attention operations is:

$$O(n \cdot w + n_g \cdot n)$$

where $n_g$ is the number of global tokens. With $w \ll n$ and $n_g \ll n$, this is linear in $n$. For $n = 4{,}096$ and $w = 512$, the cost reduction over full attention is roughly $4096 / 512 = 8\times$ per token from the local component alone.

Critically, this is not just a theoretical reduction. Longformer implemented these patterns using custom CUDA kernels that tile the local window computation across the diagonal of the attention matrix, avoiding the overhead of sparse matrix multiplication libraries which have poor GPU utilization for regular patterns. The result was that Longformer ran comfortably at 4,096 tokens — and with further work, up to 16,384 — on a single GPU.

What Longformer showed: you do not need full attention to get full-document understanding. Structured sparsity informed by task semantics is enough for most downstream applications.

What it left open: the sparsity pattern in Longformer is hand-designed for specific task types. It assumes locality — that relevant context is nearby — and uses global tokens as a workaround for the cases where it is not. But this assumption fails for tasks requiring arbitrary long-range reasoning. More fundamentally, Longformer could not prove that its sparse patterns were _sufficient_ — only that they worked empirically. A theoretical grounding for what sparse attention can and cannot express was still missing.

### BigBird: A Proof That Sparsity Is Enough

Longformer showed empirically that sparse attention works. BigBird (Zaheer et al., 2020) asked the harder question: _why_does it work? Is there a principled set of sparse patterns that is provably as expressive as full attention?

The answer required formalizing what “expressive” means. The authors drew on graph theory. View the attention pattern as a directed graph $G$ where tokens are nodes and an edge from $j$ to $i$ means token $i$ can attend to token $j$. Full attention corresponds to a complete directed graph — every token sees every other token. The question becomes: what sparse graphs preserve the computational power of the complete graph?

BigBird proved that three sparse components, combined, are sufficient to simulate any function computable by full attention — making it a _universal approximator_ in the same sense as full attention:

The first component is random attention: each token attends to $r$ randomly chosen tokens from the full sequence.

The second component is local window attention: each token attends to its $w$ nearest neighbors.

The third component is global tokens: $g$ special tokens attend to and are attended to by the entire sequence.

The theorem states that any quadratic attention pattern can be simulated by this sparse combination with only a constant overhead. The formal result is that BigBird with these three components is Turing complete — meaning it can compute anything a full transformer can compute, given sufficient depth.

The total complexity per layer is:

$$O!\left(n \cdot (r + w + g)\right)$$

where $r$, $w$, $g$ are small constants. This is strictly $O(n)$, linear in sequence length.

The empirical results matched the theory. BigBird extended BERT’s context from 512 to 4,096 tokens and achieved state-of-the-art on genomics tasks (where DNA sequences are long), multi-hop question answering, and summarization — tasks that had previously been intractable for attention-based models due to sequence length.

What BigBird gave us: not just a sparse model, but a theoretical guarantee. Sparsity is not an approximation of full attention — under the right patterns, it is equivalent to it. This justified the entire research direction.

What remained: both Longformer and BigBird still require hand-crafted sparsity patterns. The right pattern depends on the task. Furthermore, they are not easy to implement efficiently — the irregular access patterns in sparse attention fight against GPU memory layout. The community was converging on the need for a more principled, hardware-friendly approach to efficient attention. Before that, however, there was a different angle to explore: can we avoid the attention matrix altogether by approximating it with explicit mathematical structure?

### Performer: Attention Without the Matrix

Longformer and BigBird made the attention matrix sparse. The Performer (Choromanski et al., 2021) tried something more radical: eliminate the explicit matrix entirely.

Recall the attention computation:

$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) \mathbf{V}$$

The bottleneck is the $n \times n$ matrix $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top$. What if the softmax kernel $K(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) = \exp(\mathbf{q}^\top \mathbf{k} / \sqrt{d})$ could be decomposed as a dot product of explicit feature maps? That is, what if we could write:

$$K(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) \approx \phi(\mathbf{q})^\top \phi(\mathbf{k})$$

for some feature map $\phi: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^r$? Then the attention output would become:

$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) \approx \hat{\mathbf{D}}^{-1}\left(\phi(\mathbf{Q})\left(\phi(\mathbf{K})^\top \mathbf{V}\right)\right)$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{D}} = \text{diag}!\left(\phi(\mathbf{Q})\left(\phi(\mathbf{K})^\top \mathbf{1}_n\right)\right)$ is the normalizing denominator. The key observation is that $\phi(\mathbf{K})^\top \mathbf{V}$ has shape $r \times d$ — a small matrix that can be computed once and reused for all queries. The total cost drops from $O(n^2 d)$ to $O(n r d)$ where $r \ll n$.

Choromanski et al. derived the Performer using a technique called **FAVOR+** (Fast Attention Via positive Orthogonal Random features). The key insight is that the softmax kernel can be approximated using random feature maps of the form:

$$\phi(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}} \left[\exp!\left(\omega_1^\top \mathbf{x} - \tfrac{|\mathbf{x}|^2}{2}\right), \ldots, \exp!\left(\omega_m^\top \mathbf{x} - \tfrac{|\mathbf{x}|^2}{2}\right)\right]$$

where $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_m$ are drawn from an isotropic Gaussian. The approximation is unbiased (its expectation equals the true kernel value) and has lower variance when the $\omega_i$ are chosen to be _orthogonal_random features — hence the name.

The result is a linear-in-$n$ attention mechanism with provable approximation guarantees. On a sequence of 32,768 tokens, Performer runs in a fraction of the memory of standard attention and maintains strong performance on language modeling benchmarks.

The limitation, and it is significant: the softmax approximation introduces variance. For long sequences where many attention weights are very small, the random features occasionally assign non-trivial weight to irrelevant tokens. This manifests as a quality gap — on tasks requiring precise, sharp attention (code generation, multi-step reasoning), Performer can degrade noticeably. The approximation is excellent on average but not uniformly excellent.

What the Performer contributed: the conceptual move of treating attention as a kernel function and approximating it with random features. This connected the transformer literature to the classical literature on kernel methods, Gaussian processes, and random feature approximations.

What the field learned: linear attention by approximation has a real quality cost on precision tasks. The community increasingly preferred exact attention computed more cleverly (FlashAttention) over approximate attention computed cheaply. But the Performer opened theoretical avenues and its FAVOR+ framework remained a touchstone for subsequent linear attention research.

### RoFormer: Rotation as Position

We now arrive at a problem that had lurked quietly since the Transformer-XL: how do you encode position in a way that generalizes beyond the training context length?

The original sinusoidal absolute encodings simply add a fixed vector to the token embedding at each position. This means the model must learn, during training, what it means to be at position 1, position 100, position 1000. If the model is trained on sequences up to length 2,048, it has never seen position 3,000 — so its behavior there is undefined.

Relative encodings (as in Transformer-XL) are better because they encode the _distance_ between tokens rather than their absolute positions. A distance of 10 is the same whether you are at position 50 or position 5,000. But the Transformer-XL implementation required modifying the attention score computation in a complex way, adding multiple learned biases and a separate positional encoding lookup.

Su et al. (2021) noticed something more elegant. The goal of positional encoding is to make the dot product between a query at position $m$ and a key at position $n$ depend only on the relative offset $m - n$. What if we achieved this not by adding positional information to the embeddings, but by _rotating_ them in a way that encodes position geometrically?

For a 2D case, rotating vector $\mathbf{x}$ at position $m$ by angle $m\theta$ gives:

$$f(\mathbf{x}, m) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos m\theta & -\sin m\theta \ \sin m\theta & \cos m\theta \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}$$

The dot product between a rotated query at position $m$ and a rotated key at position $n$ is then:

$$\langle f(\mathbf{q}, m),; f(\mathbf{k}, n) \rangle = \mathbf{q}^\top \begin{pmatrix} \cos(m-n)\theta & -\sin(m-n)\theta \ \sin(m-n)\theta & \cos(m-n)\theta \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{k}$$

This is a function of $m - n$ only. The relative position is automatically baked in by the geometry of rotation. For a $d$-dimensional space, the construction generalizes by splitting the vector into $d/2$ pairs and applying a rotation with a different frequency $\theta_i$ to each pair:

$$\Theta = \left{\theta_i = 10000^{-2(i-1)/d} : i \in {1, \ldots, d/2}\right}$$

The full rotary matrix $\mathbf{R}_{\Theta, m}$ is then block-diagonal with $2\times2$ rotation matrices on the diagonal. The attention score computation with RoPE becomes:

$$\mathbf{q}_m^\top \mathbf{k}_n = \left(\mathbf{R}_{\Theta,m}^d \mathbf{W}_q \mathbf{x}_m\right)^\top \left(\mathbf{R}_{\Theta,n}^d \mathbf{W}_k \mathbf{x}_n\right) = \mathbf{x}_m^\top \mathbf{W}_q^\top \mathbf{R}_{\Theta,n-m}^d \mathbf{W}_k \mathbf{x}_n$$

This has several remarkable properties. The encoding is applied to queries and keys only — not values — leaving the value aggregation intact. The encoding has no additional learned parameters. The implementation requires only element-wise multiplications and additions — no matrix lookup, no positional embedding table. And crucially, the relative position is encoded implicitly by the geometry, not by an explicit lookup.

RoFormer (the paper introducing RoPE) showed that this outperformed additive relative positional encodings on machine translation and language modeling, while being simpler to implement and integrate into any transformer architecture.

Why it matters beyond a cleaner implementation: the frequency spectrum $\theta_i = 10000^{-2(i-1)/d}$ creates a structured interpolation problem that would later be exploited for context length extension — as we will see in the YaRN section. Rotary embeddings became the default positional encoding for every major subsequent model: Llama, Mistral, Gemma, Falcon, PaLM 2, and effectively all frontier models as of 2024.

What the field realized: position should be encoded in the geometry of the computation, not bolted on as an additive signal. Rotation is the natural language of relative position in a dot-product attention world.

### RMSNorm: Simplifying Stabilization

At this point in the story, a small but important question interrupts the main narrative. The Transformer uses Layer Normalization to stabilize training — without it, activations in deep networks grow or shrink exponentially through layers. The standard LayerNorm for a vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ computes:

$$\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma} \odot \gamma + \beta$$

where $\mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i$ is the mean, $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2 + \epsilon}$ is the standard deviation, and $\gamma$, $\beta$ are learned scale and shift parameters.

Zhang and Sennrich (2019) asked a question motivated by ablation: is the mean-centering step actually necessary? The hypothesis: if the network learns to use the re-scaling parameter $\gamma$ well, it should be able to handle non-centered activations. The mean subtraction is extra computation and an extra source of stochasticity during training.

Their proposal, **Root Mean Square Normalization**, drops the mean centering entirely and normalizes only by the root mean square:

$$\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\text{RMS}(\mathbf{x})} \odot \gamma, \qquad \text{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^d x_i^2 + \epsilon}$$

No mean computation. No shift parameter $\beta$. The result is approximately 10–15% faster than LayerNorm in practice on modern hardware, and empirically achieves equivalent or better training stability on language models.

This might seem like a minor optimization, but at the scale of training a 70B parameter model for trillions of tokens, shaving 10% off normalization operations — which appear in every sub-block of every layer — translates to meaningful savings. RMSNorm is now the standard normalization in Llama, Mistral, and most modern architectures.

What RMSNorm teaches: ablation studies on components considered fundamental sometimes reveal that only part of the mechanism is load-bearing. The invariance provided by mean-centering in LayerNorm apparently does not contribute to model quality — only the scale normalization does.

### SwiGLU: A Better Nonlinearity

The feed-forward network in a standard Transformer uses a ReLU activation:

$$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = \max(0,; \mathbf{x}\mathbf{W}_1 + \mathbf{b}_1),\mathbf{W}_2 + \mathbf{b}_2$$

ReLU is simple and fast but has a known problem: neurons with negative pre-activations are exactly zero and contribute no gradient. This “dying ReLU” issue was partially addressed by GELU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit), which smoothly gates activations:

$$\text{GELU}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} \cdot \Phi(\mathbf{x})$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard Gaussian CDF. GELU outperforms ReLU on language tasks and was used in GPT-2 and BERT. But Noam Shazeer (2020) found something better.

The key idea is the **Gated Linear Unit** (GLU): instead of applying a fixed nonlinearity to a single projection, apply a learned gate to control the flow of a separate linear projection:

$$\text{GLU}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{W}, \mathbf{V}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}) = \sigma(\mathbf{x}\mathbf{W} + \mathbf{b}) \odot (\mathbf{x}\mathbf{V} + \mathbf{c})$$

Replacing the sigmoid gate with Swish (also called SiLU, $\text{Swish}(x) = x \cdot \sigma(x)$) gives **SwiGLU**:

$$\text{SwiGLU}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{W}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{Swish}(\mathbf{x}\mathbf{W}) \odot (\mathbf{x}\mathbf{V})$$

The full FFN block becomes:

$$\text{FFN}_{\text{SwiGLU}}(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\text{Swish}(\mathbf{x}\mathbf{W}_1) \odot \mathbf{x}\mathbf{W}_2\right)\mathbf{W}_3$$

This requires three weight matrices instead of two. To keep parameter count constant relative to the standard FFN, the hidden dimension is typically reduced by a factor of $2/3$ — so an FFN that would have hidden dimension $4d$ with ReLU uses hidden dimension $\approx 8d/3$ with SwiGLU, rounded to a hardware-friendly multiple of 64.

The empirical improvement is consistent across model scales: on the PaLM benchmark suite, SwiGLU outperformed GELU by 0.5–1.0 perplexity points across all sizes. Shazeer described the result as “empirically superior to other variants we examined,” a characteristically understated description of what became a universal upgrade. SwiGLU is the activation in PaLM, LLaMA, Mistral, and essentially every serious large-scale model trained after 2022.

The intuition: the gating mechanism lets the network learn _which_ dimensions of the intermediate representation should be active for a given input. Rather than applying a uniform nonlinearity to all features, the gate makes the nonlinearity input-dependent. This is a form of dynamic feature selection inside the FFN.

### Chinchilla: How Much Data Does a Model Actually Need?

By 2021, scaling laws for language models seemed well-understood. Kaplan et al. (2020) at OpenAI had shown that model performance (measured by loss) scales as a power law in three quantities: the number of model parameters $N$, the number of training tokens $D$, and the amount of compute $C \approx 6ND$. The prescription implied: given a fixed compute budget, maximize the number of parameters. The data quantity should roughly equal the parameter count — or so the Kaplan laws suggested.

This led to a generation of over-parameterized, undertrained models. GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens), Gopher (280B parameters, 300B tokens), and Megatron-Turing NLG (530B parameters, 270B tokens) were all trained with roughly 1–2 tokens per parameter. The implicit assumption was that training data was cheap and model size was the primary driver of capability.

Hoffmann et al. (2022) at DeepMind ran a systematic experiment to challenge this assumption. They trained over 400 models ranging from 70M to 16B parameters on datasets ranging from 5B to 400B tokens, measuring final validation loss across all combinations. From this grid of experiments, they fit a more careful scaling law.

The form they fitted for compute-optimal training was:

$$L(N, D) = \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta} + L_\infty$$

where $A$, $B$, $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $L_\infty$ are constants fitted from data. The key finding: $\alpha \approx \beta \approx 0.34$, meaning that model size and data quantity contribute roughly _equally_ to reducing loss per unit of compute. Neither should be prioritized over the other.

The compute-optimal allocation follows:

$$N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.5}, \qquad D_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.5}$$

Doubling your compute budget should be split roughly equally between a larger model and more training tokens — not funneled entirely into a larger model.

As a proof of concept, they trained Chinchilla: 70 billion parameters on 1.4 trillion tokens. Compared to Gopher (280B parameters, 300B tokens), Chinchilla used 4× fewer parameters but 4.6× more data — the same total compute budget. Chinchilla outperformed Gopher on virtually every benchmark, often by large margins. It also outperformed GPT-3, Megatron-Turing, and all other contemporaneous models despite being smaller than most of them.

The practical consequence was immediate and transformative. Chinchilla showed that the entire field had been training models incorrectly. Models like Llama were subsequently trained at near-Chinchilla-optimal ratios — Llama 1 used 7B, 13B, 33B, and 65B parameter models on 1T tokens each, reversing the previous bias toward large undertrained models.

What Chinchilla revealed that no individual model could have: the _shape_ of the loss landscape in $(N, D)$ space. Scaling in one dimension without the other is wasteful. A 175B model trained on 300B tokens is far from its optimal loss — more training data would help it more than more parameters would.

The lasting implication: data is not a cheap commodity to be consumed to serve model size. It is a resource of equal value, and frontier labs have since invested enormously in data quality, curation, and quantity — with the data budgets of models like Llama 3 (15T tokens), Gemini, and GPT-4 reflecting the lesson Chinchilla taught.

### FlashAttention: IO-Aware Exact Attention

We have arrived at the bottleneck that turned out to be engineering as much as mathematics. By 2022, the community had accepted that extending context windows required reducing the cost of attention. Sparse and approximate methods worked, but they either required task-specific engineering (Longformer) or introduced approximation error (Performer). What if exact, full attention could simply be made faster?

Dao et al. (2022) identified the real bottleneck, which was not _flops_ but _memory bandwidth_. Modern GPUs have two tiers of memory: large, slow HBM (High Bandwidth Memory, tens of GB, ~900 GB/s bandwidth) and small, fast SRAM (on-chip shared memory, ~100 KB per SM, ~19 TB/s bandwidth — roughly 20× faster). Standard attention makes multiple round trips between them:

Round trip 1: load $\mathbf{Q}$, $\mathbf{K}$ from HBM, compute $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{QK}^\top$, write $\mathbf{S}$ back to HBM. Round trip 2: load $\mathbf{S}$ from HBM, compute softmax $\mathbf{P} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{S})$, write $\mathbf{P}$ back to HBM. Round trip 3: load $\mathbf{P}$, $\mathbf{V}$ from HBM, compute $\mathbf{O} = \mathbf{PV}$, write $\mathbf{O}$ to HBM.

Each of the intermediate matrices $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{P}$ has size $n \times n$. Writing and reading them to HBM costs $O(n^2)$ bandwidth. For $n = 4096$, this is roughly 128MB per head per forward pass — and doing it three times at 900 GB/s introduces a latency that completely dominates the compute time.

The solution is to process attention in tiles that fit in SRAM, never materializing the full $n \times n$ matrix. The obstacle is the softmax: to normalize any score, you seem to need the sum of all scores in the row. This is the online softmax insight from earlier — the key algorithmic contribution.

Define the running statistics for the softmax of a partial sequence of scores. For scores processed in chunks, maintain:

$$m^{(j)} = \max(m^{(j-1)},; \tilde{m}^{(j)}), \qquad \ell^{(j)} = e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}} \ell^{(j-1)} + e^{\tilde{m}^{(j)} - m^{(j)}} \tilde{\ell}^{(j)}$$

where $\tilde{m}^{(j)}$ and $\tilde{\ell}^{(j)}$ are the max and sum-of-exponentials of the current chunk. The output accumulator is updated as:

$$\mathbf{o}^{(j)} = \frac{e^{m^{(j-1)} - m^{(j)}} \ell^{(j-1)} \mathbf{o}^{(j-1)} + e^{\tilde{m}^{(j)} - m^{(j)}} \tilde{\mathbf{o}}^{(j)}}{\ell^{(j)}}$$

After processing all chunks, $\mathbf{o}^{(\text{final})}$ is exactly equal to the output of standard attention. No approximation. The running max correction ensures numerical stability.

The result: FlashAttention performs the entire attention computation — forward and backward — with only $O(n)$ HBM reads and writes, reducing IO cost by 5–20× over standard attention. Wall-clock time improvements were 2–4× on sequences of 1K–4K tokens, growing larger with sequence length.

This is the thing we discovered: **IO-aware exact attention via tiled online softmax**. It is not an approximation. It is the same computation, restructured to respect the memory hierarchy of the hardware it runs on.

What FlashAttention unlocked: training at 2–4× the previous speed on sequences up to ~8K tokens. Memory usage dropped from $O(n^2)$ to $O(n)$, making 8K and 16K contexts feasible on single GPUs. It became the universal attention backend — integrated into PyTorch, used by every major lab, and forming the substrate on which all subsequent context extension work was built.

What it left: the quadratic _compute_ cost remained. FlashAttention made the IO efficient; the number of flops was unchanged. For $n = 1{,}000{,}000$, even at zero IO cost, the compute is still $10^{12}$ operations per head per layer. A different approach was needed for extreme context.

### FlashAttention-2: Maximizing GPU Utilization

A year after FlashAttention, Dao (2023) returned with a sharper analysis of why the original was still leaving performance on the table.

FlashAttention’s tiling algorithm had a subtle load-imbalance: the outer loop was over query blocks (parallelized across GPU thread blocks) and the inner loop was over key-value blocks (sequential within a thread block). This meant each thread block was responsible for the full inner loop over all KV tiles — a large serial workload. On an A100 GPU with 108 streaming multiprocessors, if the number of query blocks was small (e.g., a short sequence or few heads), many SMs sat idle.

FlashAttention-2 restructured the loop ordering to swap inner and outer loops, enabling better parallelism across the KV dimension. It also minimized unnecessary non-matmul operations (rescaling steps in the softmax accumulator), reducing them to the minimum required for correctness. The number of HBM accesses was further reduced by avoiding writing intermediate values to registers unnecessarily.

The result: FlashAttention-2 achieves 50–73% of the theoretical peak FLOPS on A100 GPUs, compared to 25–35% for FlashAttention-1 and under 25% for standard implementations. On long sequences (4K–16K tokens), it is roughly 2× faster than FlashAttention-1 and 6–9× faster than the baseline PyTorch implementation.

Numerically, for a sequence of length $n$, the number of HBM reads/writes in FlashAttention-2 is:

$$\Theta!\left(\n d\right)$$

where $d$ is the head dimension — strictly linear in $n$, with a smaller constant than FA-1 due to reduced register traffic.

FlashAttention-2 also added native support for multi-query attention and grouped-query attention (which we will see shortly) — by design, not as a post-hoc adaptation. This tight co-design between the attention variant and the kernel implementation is why FA-2 became the standard reference implementation for all serious transformer training as of 2023.

### Multi-Query Attention: Sharing Keys and Values

We now face the inference bottleneck. At training time, the KV cache does not exist — you compute attention over the full sequence once. But at inference time, to generate token $t + 1$, you must attend over all $t$ previous tokens. Without caching, you would recompute all $t$ key and value vectors from scratch each step — at $O(t)$ cost per token, the total generation cost would be $O(T^2)$ for a $T$-token response.

The KV cache solves this: store the key and value tensors for all previous tokens. Each new token pays $O(t)$ for an attention scan over the cached KVs, and $O(1)$ to update the cache with its own KV pair. Total generation cost is $O(T^2)$ in attention scans but $O(T)$ in _additional_ computation per token.

The problem: the cache size. For a model with $L$ layers, $H$ attention heads, head dimension $d$, and context length $n$, the KV cache requires:

$$\text{Memory} = 2 \times n \times L \times H \times d \times \text{bytes/param}$$

For a 70B parameter model (L=80, H=64, d=128) at FP16 (2 bytes) with $n = 128{,}000$ context tokens:

$$2 \times 128000 \times 80 \times 64 \times 128 \times 2 \approx 419 \text{ GB}$$

This exceeds the VRAM of any single GPU. The model weights themselves are about 140GB. The KV cache is three times the model size. Something has to give.

Shazeer (2019) proposed the simplest possible answer: instead of one K and V head per query head, share a _single_ K and V across all query heads. This is **Multi-Query Attention** (MQA):

$$\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_i^Q,; \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}^K,; \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}^V)$$

All $H$ query heads use the same key and value projections $\mathbf{W}^K$ and $\mathbf{W}^V$. The KV cache shrinks by a factor of $H$:

$$\text{MQA cache} = 2 \times n \times L \times 1 \times d \times \text{bytes/param}$$

For the 70B example, this is now $\approx 6.5$GB instead of 419GB — a 64× reduction. At inference, the speedup is dramatic because KV cache reading is often the bottleneck on memory-bandwidth-limited hardware.

The quality cost exists but is modest. MQA was adopted in PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022) and showed competitive results despite the extreme parameter sharing. The intuition is that the different query heads can attend to different aspects of the same key-value representation — the heads remain differentiated through their query projections even when they share KV.

### Grouped-Query Attention: Interpolating for Quality

Multi-Query Attention’s quality degradation, while modest, was real and inconsistent across tasks. For tasks requiring fine-grained token discrimination — code generation, precise factual recall — the single shared KV head was a bottleneck. The query heads could not attend to meaningfully different aspects of the sequence because they all saw the same key-value representation.

Ainslie et al. (2023) proposed **Grouped-Query Attention** (GQA), which interpolates between MHA (Multi-Head Attention, one KV per query head) and MQA (one KV for all query heads). The $H$ query heads are divided into $G$ groups, with one shared KV head per group:

$$\text{head}_{i,g} = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_{i,g}^Q,; \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_g^K,; \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}_g^V)$$

where $g \in {1, \ldots, G}$ indexes the group and $i \in {1, \ldots, H/G}$ indexes the head within the group. When $G = H$ this reduces to standard MHA. When $G = 1$ this reduces to MQA.

The KV cache size is:

$$\text{GQA cache} = 2 \times n \times L \times G \times d \times \text{bytes/param}$$

With $G = 8$ and $H = 64$ (as in Llama 3), this is an 8× reduction versus MHA while maintaining substantially better quality than MQA. The key empirical finding in the GQA paper: models trained with GQA can nearly match MHA quality when converted from MHA checkpoints via a brief fine-tuning phase (uptraining) with only a fraction of the original compute budget.

GQA is now the standard in production: Llama 2, Llama 3, Mistral, Gemma, and most frontier models use it. The tradeoff point $G = 8$ appears to sit in the regime where query-head differentiation is retained (each group of 8 queries shares one KV, but 8 such KV heads cover a wide range of key patterns) while the memory savings are substantial.

### Mixture of Experts: Scaling Without Paying Full Cost

There is a fundamental tension in dense transformer scaling: every token activates every parameter. A 70B parameter model processes every single token through all 70B parameters — even if the token is a punctuation mark that needs no deep reasoning. As models grow to hundreds of billions of parameters, this becomes extraordinarily wasteful.

The Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture, with roots in the 1990s work of Jacobs, Jordan, Nowlan, and Hinton, was revived for transformers by Shazeer et al. (2017) and scaled dramatically by Fedus, Zoph, and Shazeer (2022) in the Switch Transformer. The idea is to replace the dense FFN in each transformer layer with a collection of $E$ independent FFN “experts” and a learned router that selects which expert(s) to activate for each token.

For a token representation $\mathbf{x}$, the router produces a probability distribution over experts:

$$\mathbf{p}(\mathbf{x}) = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{W}_r \mathbf{x})$$

The output selects the top-$k$ experts by weight:

$$y = \sum_{i \in \text{Top-}k(\mathbf{p})} p_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \text{FFN}_i(\mathbf{x})$$

Most modern MoE models use $k = 2$ (two experts per token). This means each token activates only $2/E$ of the FFN parameters, dramatically reducing the active parameter count per forward pass.

The catch: expert collapse. Without constraint, the router learns to always prefer a small number of popular experts, while the rest are never trained. To prevent this, a load-balancing auxiliary loss is added during training:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = \alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_{i=1}^E f_i \cdot P_i$$

where $f_i$ is the fraction of tokens dispatched to expert $i$ in a batch, $P_i$ is the average router probability assigned to expert $i$, and $\alpha$ is a small scalar (typically 0.01). This penalizes configurations where experts receive unequal load.

The scaling argument: a model with $E = 8$ experts and $k = 2$ has the parameter count of a model with roughly 4× the FFN size, but the compute cost (FLOPs) of activating only 2 out of 8 experts — equal to the original dense model. You get more parameters for the same compute. Alternatively: you can match a dense model’s compute while training a much larger parameter count on more data.

Mixtral 8×7B (Mistral AI, 2023) demonstrated this vividly: 8 experts of 7B parameters each gives a total of ~47B parameters, but each token activates only 2 experts, so the active parameter count during inference is ~13B. Mixtral 8×7B matched or exceeded Llama 2 70B (a dense model) on most benchmarks while being roughly 5× faster at inference. The GPT-4 architecture is widely reported (though not officially confirmed) to use a MoE structure at scale.

The engineering challenges are real. Experts must be distributed across devices, and routing different tokens to different devices creates variable communication patterns. Token dropping (when too many tokens want the same expert in a given batch) degrades quality. The training instability of the load-balancing mechanism requires careful hyperparameter tuning. But for training frontier models where compute efficiency is paramount, MoE is increasingly the architecture of choice.

### YaRN: Position Interpolation at Scale

FlashAttention solved the IO bottleneck. GQA solved the KV cache bottleneck. But there was still the position wall: RoPE-based models trained at context length 4,096 degraded catastrophically when asked to process sequences of 32,768 or 131,072 tokens. The rotation angles at large positions simply fell outside the training distribution.

The naive fix, introduced by Chen et al. (2023), is **position interpolation**: scale all position indices by $L_{\text{train}} / L_{\text{target}}$ before computing the RoPE rotations. A position that would be $m = 100{,}000$ in a 1M-token context is mapped to:

$$m’ = m \cdot \frac{L_{\text{train}}}{L_{\text{target}}} = 100000 \cdot \frac{4096}{1000000} \approx 0.41$$

This keeps positions within the training range. But it has a problem: all positions are compressed equally, including short-range positions that were already well-represented. High-frequency components of the RoPE embedding (small $\theta_i$ in the frequency spectrum) can distinguish tokens at distances of 1–10 tokens and are critical for local syntax. Compressing them reduces this discriminative power for short distances.

Peng et al. (2023) developed **YaRN** (Yet Another RoPE extensioN), which applies different scaling to different frequency components based on the “wavelength” of each RoPE dimension. Define the wavelength $\lambda_i = 2\pi \cdot \theta_i^{-1}$ for the $i$-th frequency component. Components with $\lambda_i < L_{\text{train}}$ are well within the training distribution and should be interpolated (compressed). Components with $\lambda_i > L_{\text{target}}$ are extrapolated even in the target context and should be left alone. Components in between get a ramp interpolation.

Formally, define a scaling factor $s$ for each frequency dimension using a ramp function $\gamma$:

$$s_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \lambda_i < \lambda_{\text{low}} \ \frac{1}{t} \cdot \frac{\lambda_i - \lambda_{\text{low}}}{\lambda_{\text{high}} - \lambda_{\text{low}}} + \frac{1}{\text{scale}} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{\lambda_i - \lambda_{\text{low}}}{\lambda_{\text{high}} - \lambda_{\text{low}}}\right) & \text{if } \lambda_{\text{low}} \leq \lambda_i \leq \lambda_{\text{high}} \ \frac{1}{\text{scale}} & \text{if } \lambda_i > \lambda_{\text{high}} \end{cases}$$

where $\lambda_{\text{low}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{high}}$ are thresholds separating the three regimes, and scale is the overall context extension factor $L_{\text{target}} / L_{\text{train}}$.

Additionally, YaRN applies a temperature correction to the attention scores. When positions are interpolated, all pairwise distances are compressed, making the attention distribution more uniform (lower entropy). To compensate, the attention temperature is scaled by:

$$\text{softmax}!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_m^\top \mathbf{k}_n}{\sqrt{d} \cdot t}\right), \qquad t = 0.1 \ln(s) + 1$$

where $s$ is the extension scale. This sharpens the attention distribution back to its original entropy level.

The result: with only 0.1% of the original pretraining compute for fine-tuning at the extended context length, YaRN extended Llama 2 (originally trained at 4K) to 128K context with perplexity at 64K tokens nearly matching a model natively trained at 64K. This made context extension almost free — labs could take any existing RoPE-based model and apply YaRN fine-tuning to dramatically extend its context.

### Ring Attention: Distributing Context Across Devices

By 2023, the algorithmic bottlenecks had been managed: FlashAttention removed the IO wall, GQA managed KV cache memory, YaRN solved position generalization. The remaining obstacle to 1M+ context was hardware: a single GPU’s SRAM simply cannot hold even the working memory for a tiled forward pass over 1M tokens. FlashAttention tiles the $n \times n$ attention within a device; but if the sequence itself doesn’t fit on a device, you need a different parallelism strategy.

Liu et al. (2023) introduced **Ring Attention**. The sequence is partitioned into $N_d$ blocks across $N_d$ devices arranged in a logical ring. Each device $k$ holds tokens $\left[\frac{k \cdot n}{N_d}, \frac{(k+1) \cdot n}{N_d}\right)$.

Attention requires each query to see all keys and values. Ring Attention achieves this through a rotation protocol. At each step $s$ of the ring, device $k$ holds the KV block from device $(k - s) \bmod N_d$. It computes the partial attention between its local Q block and the currently held KV block, accumulating the online softmax statistics. Simultaneously, it sends the KV block to the next device in the ring. After $N_d$ steps, every device has seen every KV block and holds the correct output for its portion of the sequence.

The communication pattern is a ring-allreduce of the KV blocks. At each step, each device sends one KV block of size $\frac{2nd}{N_d}$ elements and receives one KV block of the same size. The total communication volume per device is:

$$\text{Communication} = 2 \cdot \frac{n \cdot d}{N_d} \cdot N_d = 2nd$$

independent of $N_d$. More devices do not increase communication per device. The compute per device is:

$$\text{FLOPs} = \frac{n^2 d}{N_d}$$

which scales down linearly with the number of devices. Both the memory per device ($O(n/N_d)$) and the compute per device ($O(n^2/N_d)$) decrease linearly with the number of devices.

Crucially, the communication and compute can be _overlapped_: while device $k$ is computing attention between its Q block and the current KV block, it simultaneously sends the KV block to device $k+1$. If the compute time per step is greater than the communication time — which it is for sufficiently long sequences — the communication is fully hidden. Ring Attention achieves near-linear scaling efficiency with the number of devices.

With 512 devices and Ring Attention, a context of $512 \times 8192 = 4{,}194{,}304$ tokens becomes tractable. This is the architectural basis for the 1M+ context windows in Gemini 1.5 and Claude’s long-context variants.

### Speculative Decoding: Making Inference Fast

We have spent this entire chapter making the _forward pass_ more efficient — attention cheaper, caches smaller, context longer. But there is a different problem at inference that none of these solutions addresses: autoregressive generation is inherently sequential. To generate token $t$, you must have already generated tokens $1$ through $t-1$. Each token requires one full forward pass through the model. A 70B parameter model generating 1,000 tokens requires 1,000 sequential forward passes — there is no way to parallelize across the output sequence.

Or is there?

Leviathan, Kalman, and Matias (2023) and Chen et al. (2023) independently discovered **Speculative Decoding**. The key observation: verifying whether a proposed sequence is likely under a large model is faster than generating that sequence with the large model, because verification can be done in a single parallel forward pass.

The algorithm uses a small “draft” model $M_q$ (fast, cheap, approximate) and the large “target” model $M_p$ (slow, expensive, accurate). The protocol is:

Step 1: Draft model generates $\gamma$ speculative tokens $x_1, \ldots, x_\gamma$ autoregressively (fast, because $M_q$ is small).

Step 2: Run the large model on the full sequence including the $\gamma$ draft tokens — _in a single parallel forward pass_. This gives the target model’s distribution at every draft position simultaneously.

Step 3: Accept or reject each draft token using the following rule. For draft token $x_t$ with draft probability $q(x_t)$ and target probability $p(x_t)$:

$$\text{Accept } x_t \text{ with probability } \min!\left(1,; \frac{p(x_t)}{q(x_t)}\right)$$

If accepted, move to the next draft token. If rejected at position $t$, discard $x_t, \ldots, x_\gamma$ and sample a correction token from a modified distribution:

$$p’(x) \propto \max(0,; p(x) - q(x))$$

Step 4: The output distribution is identical to sampling directly from $M_p$. The acceptance criterion ensures no quality degradation — speculative decoding is lossless.

The speedup comes from the parallelism in Step 2. If all $\gamma$ draft tokens are accepted, the large model has effectively generated $\gamma$ tokens for the cost of one forward pass. The expected number of tokens generated per forward pass of the large model is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{tokens per step}] = \frac{1 - \alpha^{\gamma+1}}{1 - \alpha}$$

where $\alpha = \mathbb{E}[\min(1, p/q)]$ is the expected acceptance rate. For a well-matched draft model, $\alpha \approx 0.7$–$0.9$, and with $\gamma = 4$–$8$ draft tokens, the speedup is typically 2–3× with no quality loss.

The key constraint is that the large model’s forward pass on $\gamma + 1$ tokens costs only slightly more than on 1 token — which is true when the model is memory-bandwidth-bound (as most are during inference), because the dominant cost is loading the model weights, not the compute. Each forward pass loads ~70GB of weights regardless of sequence length (within reason), so processing 5 tokens costs barely more than processing 1.

Speculative decoding is now ubiquitous in production inference stacks. Google uses it in Gemini inference. Anthropic uses it in Claude. The draft model can be trained to minimize the gap with the target model, or it can be a smaller version of the same model family (e.g., a 7B draft for a 70B target). Some implementations use even simpler “draft” strategies — n-gram lookup, cached common phrases — to reduce draft latency to near-zero.

### FlashAttention-3: Hardware Co-Design for the H100 Era

FlashAttention-2 was designed for the A100 GPU. The H100, released in 2022 and deployed at scale in 2023, introduced architectural changes that FA-2 was not designed to exploit.

The H100’s key additions: the Tensor Memory Accelerator (TMA) unit, which can issue asynchronous memory copies between HBM and SRAM without stalling the main computation pipeline; the WGMMA (Warp Group Matrix Multiply-Accumulate) instruction, which issues matrix multiplications asynchronously to tensor cores; and FP8 precision (8-bit floating point) support with higher throughput than FP16.

Shah et al. (2024) designed FlashAttention-3 to exploit these specifically.

The first optimization is _asynchronous pipelining_. In FA-2, loading a tile of K/V from HBM and computing the attention matmul with that tile happen sequentially. In FA-3, these are pipelined: while the GPU computes attention with tile $j$, it asynchronously loads tile $j+1$ using TMA. When the compute for tile $j$ completes, tile $j+1$ is already in SRAM. The memory latency is hidden behind compute.

The second optimization is _2-stage tiling for softmax_. The online softmax update (rescaling the accumulator by $e^{m_{\text{old}} - m_{\text{new}}}$) involves exponential operations that are expensive relative to the matmul throughput. FA-3 separates the raw attention score accumulation (pure matmul, dispatched to tensor cores via WGMMA) from the rescaling step (handled asynchronously on CUDA cores), keeping the tensor cores occupied without waiting for the softmax correction.

The third optimization is _FP8 attention_. The forward pass computes QK$^\top$ in FP8, accepting some precision loss in the attention scores for a 2× throughput increase. A careful analysis shows that attention scores are robust to FP8 quantization because they are subsequently passed through softmax — small errors in the pre-softmax logits are amplified or suppressed predictably. The V matrix multiplication is kept in higher precision.

The combined result on H100 SXM5: FlashAttention-3 achieves 740–1200 TFLOPS on FP16 attention, compared to 350–400 TFLOPS for FA-2 — a 1.5–2× speedup. It reaches 75% of H100’s peak theoretical FLOPS on attention, compared to 35% for FA-2. For context lengths of 64K–128K, the speedup over FA-2 is closer to 2×.

This is where the stack stands at the frontier. FlashAttention-3 as the kernel. Ring Attention for distribution across devices. YaRN for position extension. GQA for KV cache management. Speculative decoding for inference throughput. MoE for parameter efficiency. SwiGLU + RMSNorm for training stability. RoPE for positional encoding. The modern large language model is not a single idea but a carefully assembled stack, each layer solving the problem that the layer below it created.

The next problem waiting at the frontier: even with 1M context, models fail to reliably use information distributed throughout that context. The binding constraint has moved from infrastructure to understanding — from “can the model attend to distant tokens” to “does the model _know_ what to do with what it attends to.” That is where the next chapter begins.
