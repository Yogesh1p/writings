## What Is H. pylori and Why Is It Getting Worse?

_Helicobacter pylori_ is a bacterial infection that lives in the stomach lining. It is one of the most common bacterial infections in the world, and projections suggest it will affect even more people in the coming years — particularly in South Asia, where sanitation, shared food, and water sources create easy transmission routes.

Symptoms include bloating, burning stomach pain, nausea, and in worse cases, ulcers and gastric bleeding. Many people carry it without knowing. Others, like me, find out only after months of worsening symptoms.

Here is the problem nobody tells you upfront: **standard treatments fail a lot.** In South India especially, antibiotic resistance rates are high. If your first or second regimen didn’t work, you are not alone — and you are not out of options.

**H. pylori messes with your brain — this is documented science, not imagination.**

The depression, numbness, and emotional flatness many people feel during infection is not just from being unwell. H. pylori infection causes fluctuations in serotonin, dopamine, ghrelin, and cortisol levels in the circulatory system, resulting in damage to the central nervous system and corresponding psychological symptoms. [Wisconsin Medical Journal](https://wmjonline.org/123no1/karamanakos/)

H. pylori disrupts gut microbiota composition and increases intestinal permeability, which directly influences the production and metabolism of serotonin, dopamine, and GABA — the neurotransmitters that regulate mood. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7779136/)

It goes even further. H. pylori infection triggers chronic inflammation that damages the blood-brain barrier, and these processes destroy brain neurons including dopamine-releasing neurons. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metronidazole)

H. pylori has also been linked to poor cognition, neurological impairment, cerebrovascular disease, and is recognised as a significant risk factor for dementia and Parkinson’s disease. [Psychiatry Times](https://www.psychiatrictimes.com/view/psychiatric-adverse-effects-antibiotics)

The good news is that eradicating H. pylori reverses much of this. Rebuilding your gut flora after treatment is not just about digestion — it is directly about restoring the neurotransmitter systems the bacteria spent months disrupting.

## Why First and Second Line Treatments Fail in South India

South India has documented high resistance to:

- **Metronidazole** (when used alone or in standard triple therapy)
    
- **Clarithromycin**
    
- **Amoxicillin** in some strains
    

This is why triple therapy — the most commonly prescribed regimen — has failure rates of 20–40% or higher in our region. If you’ve failed once or twice, it’s likely a resistance issue, not something you did wrong.

The solution is to hit the bacteria from multiple angles simultaneously, which is exactly what bismuth quadruple therapy does.

## The Regimen That Worked: Bismuth Quadruple Therapy (14 Days)

- **Vonoprazan 20mg** (twice daily) — powerful, fast acid suppression
    
- **Bismuth Subcitrate 120mg** (four times daily) — coats stomach lining + direct antibacterial action
    
- **Tetracycline 500mg** (four times daily) — inhibits bacterial protein synthesis
    
- **Metronidazole 400mg** (four times daily) — in combination with bismuth, has significantly better efficacy against H. pylori
    

**Why Vonoprazan instead of a standard PPI?** Vonoprazan is a potassium-competitive acid blocker — it works faster and maintains acid suppression more reliably than older PPIs like omeprazole. This matters because the antibiotics only work properly in a low-acid environment.

**Side effects I experienced:** No sleep for the entire 14 days, nausea, near-zero energy, and mood swings that persisted for about a month after finishing. It is a hard 14 days. But it worked.

**Avoid sun exposure for the entire 14 days.** Tetracycline causes photosensitivity — your skin becomes highly sensitive to UV light and even moderate sun can cause severe sunburn or skin rashes. If you must go outside, cover up fully or use sunscreen. This is especially important in South India where sun intensity is high.

Avoid kefir, curd, milk any diary products during the treatment

## Managing Pain and Symptoms before the Treatment

These helped me get through it:

- **Sucralfate + Oxetacaine suspension** — coats and numbs the stomach lining, genuinely helpful for pain
    
- **Gaviscon** — antacid for reflux
    
- **Pantoprazole-D slow release (Pantop-D)** — additional acid control between Vonoprazan doses
    
- **IV Pantoprazole** — for severe pain episodes, this can be given by injection at a clinic
    

Even after successful eradication, gastritis can persist for weeks or months. Continue a PPI during this period. The goal is to protect the mucosal layer so it heals fully and doesn’t allow recolonisation.

**Why no dairy?** Dairy binds to tetracycline and significantly reduces its absorption. This is not optional — dairy during treatment could be why some people’s regimens fail.

**Why stay upright after tetracycline?** Tetracycline can cause esophageal ulceration if it sits in the esophagus. Always take it with a full glass of water and stay upright for at least 30 minutes.

## Probiotics: What Worked and What Didn’t

Getting this right matters. Probiotics taken too close to antibiotics get wiped out — useless. Take them at least 2 hours away from any antibiotic dose.

**What didn’t work for me:** _Limosilactobacillus reuteri 25 Billion CFU_ — too strong, caused significant vulnerability and discomfort. Stopped after 7 doses.

**What worked:** _Lactobacillus reuteri DSM 17938_ — this specific strain has clinical evidence behind it for H. pylori support. Tolerable, well-studied. I started from Day 7 of the antibiotic course and continued for a full month after finishing.

## Diet During and After Treatment

### Enzyme-Rich Foods (Aid Digestion When Your Gut Is Struggling)

- **Papaya** — papain enzyme, breaks down proteins, very gentle on the stomach
    
- **Pineapple** — bromelain, anti-inflammatory and aids protein digestion
    
- **Kiwi** — actinidin, helps protein breakdown in the small intestine
    
- **Raw honey** — amylase enzyme, supports carbohydrate digestion and probiotic growth
    
- **Bananas** — amylase and resistant starch, prebiotic, easy to digest
    
- **Avocado** — lipase enzyme, healthy fats, high fibre
    
- **Kefir and yogurt** — live cultures, help repopulate gut flora (after treatment, not during — avoid dairy during the 14-day course)
    

### Vegetables and Sulforaphane

Eat vegetable curries daily. Specifically, prioritise **cruciferous vegetables**:

- Broccoli
    
- Cabbage
    
- Cauliflower
    

These contain **sulforaphane**, a compound that has been studied for its direct inhibitory effect on H. pylori and its ability to reduce gastric inflammation. In South Indian cooking, these can easily be made into sabzis or added to dal — you don’t need to eat them raw.

### Anti-Inflammatory Eating

**Switch to cold-pressed oils.** Refined oils are processed at high heat and stripped of their natural compounds — they also promote systemic inflammation, which is the last thing a healing gut needs. Cold-pressed options like cold-pressed coconut oil, groundnut oil, or sesame oil are widely available in South India and far better for gut health.

**Avoid refined oils entirely** during treatment and recovery if possible.

### Omega-3 for Inflammation

Oily fish are your best natural source of omega-3:

- Salmon
    
- Vanjaram (Seer fish)
    
- Bangda (Mackerel)
    

Eat these 2–3 times a week. If your diet doesn’t allow regular fish, use a **verified fish oil supplement**. I used one verified by Trustified — quality verification matters here because many fish oil supplements on the market are oxidised or underdosed.

The goal with omega-3 is to balance your omega-6 to omega-3 ratio, which in most Indian diets is heavily skewed toward omega-6 (pro-inflammatory). Getting more omega-3 helps shift this balance and supports mucosal healing.

## After Treatment: Protecting the Mucosal Layer

L-Glutamine is the primary fuel source for intestinal epithelial cells, so it directly supports the rebuilding of the gut lining after the damage from both H. pylori and the antibiotic course.

[zincasin plus](https://www.amazon.in/dp/B0B8SMFVB9?ref=ppx_yo2ov_dt_b_fed_asin_title&th=1) this supplement too.

Eradication is not the end. The mucosal lining of your stomach has been damaged — both by the bacteria over time and by the antibiotics themselves. This is why many people still feel pain and discomfort after a successful course.

What I focused on after treatment:

1. **Continue a PPI** (like Pantop-D) for several weeks to allow the lining to heal
    
2. **Keep taking L. reuteri DSM 17938** for at least a month
    
3. **Eat enzyme-rich foods and vegetable curries daily**
    
4. **Take Zincasin Plus** for mucosal recovery
    
5. **Avoid NSAIDs** (like ibuprofen) — these directly damage the stomach lining
    
6. **Avoid alcohol** completely during recovery( I’m a teetotaler)
    
7. **Maintain cold-pressed oils only** in your cooking
    
8. **Stay on omega-3** through fish or verified supplements
    

The mucosal layer is your long-term protection. Once it’s healthy and intact, H. pylori has no surface to colonise. That is the real goal.
