There is a concept in reinforcement learning called a value function. It is the internal mechanism an agent uses to estimate how much reward any given state or action is worth. The agent doesn’t pursue things because they are inherently good. It pursues them because its value function says they are good. Change the value function, and you change the entire behavior of the system — not by altering the world, but by altering what the agent perceives the world to be worth.

Humans work analogously. Not identically — the analogy has limits that matter and I’ll mark them where they appear. But the structural parallel is useful: behavior is downstream of valuation, and valuation is a mechanism, not a perception. We do not see value in things the way we see color. We assign it, through processes that are largely invisible to us, and then experience the result as if it were a property of the object.

This essay is an attempt to map those processes — where the value function comes from, how it breaks down, and what it would actually mean to take authorship of your own.



## Part I: Value Is Not Received — It Is Assigned

The same song, performed by the same singer with identical skill: unnoticed in a crowded subway, received as world-class in a concert hall with 20,000 people. The music didn’t change. What changed was the narrative architecture surrounding it — the crowd, the staging, the ticket price, the months of anticipation. That scaffolding tells you what the experience is worth before you hear the first note, and it produces genuine felt value, not just cognitive acknowledgment of value.

This is not a bug or a failure of rationality. It is how valuation works in a social species. Humans use collective behavior as evidence of worth. When many people treat something as valuable — pay for it, wait in line for it, talk about it — that consensus functions as a signal, and the signal updates felt importance. The brain constructs value from available evidence rather than reading it directly from objects, and social proof is among the most powerful evidence available precisely because, across most of evolutionary history, “many people want this” was a reliable proxy for “this is genuinely useful.”

The problem is that the proxy and the underlying quality it tracks have come apart in the modern environment. Social proof now travels faster than direct experience. Narrative scales in ways that reality doesn’t. And so perceived value and experienced value diverge — sometimes narrowly, sometimes completely.

This divergence is the source of most misdirected effort in adult life. Not laziness, not weakness of will, not failure of intelligence. A value function that was calibrated on social signal rather than direct experience, running on inputs that were never verified.


## Part II: The Inheritance Problem

Most of the value function was not built by you. It was absorbed before you had the machinery to evaluate it.

From parents: what kinds of work are respectable, what constitutes a good life, what security looks like. From teachers: what counts as intellectual achievement and what counts as failure. From peer groups: what is desirable, what is embarrassing, what signals competence or belonging. From culture: a ranked hierarchy of goals — wealth, status, productivity, credentials — that arrive pre-weighted and pre-sorted.

None of this is delivered as opinion. It arrives as the texture of the world. The things that are treated as obviously important feel obviously important, because you absorbed the treatment and the importance together, before they could be separated. By the time you’re old enough to ask “why does this matter,” the answer is already built into the felt landscape of experience. You don’t interrogate it because it doesn’t present itself as a claim. It presents itself as a fact about the world.

The mechanism is visible in any situation where high narrative value meets direct experience for the first time. Think of the person who has spent years building toward a specific kind of recognition — a promotion, a credential, a creative milestone — and finally receives it. The moment itself is often strangely flat. Not because the achievement isn’t real, but because the felt importance was never primarily about the thing. It was about the story of getting it, the identity the pursuit was supposed to confirm, the relief that was supposed to follow. The arrival reveals that the emotional architecture was built around the striving, not the destination. Without the striving there is nothing holding the structure up.

The disorientation this produces is not ordinary disappointment. The world disappoints people constantly without destabilizing them. This particular flatness destabilizes because it exposes something structural: the importance you felt was tracking the narrative, not the reality. You pursued a social construction and experienced it as pursuing a fact about the world.

The inherited value function ran correctly by its own logic. It was calibrated on the wrong inputs.



## Part III: The Hidden Equation

Beneath inherited value systems there is usually a logical structure that, once visible, explains a lot of otherwise puzzling behavior.

The implicit equation runs like this:

**Hard → Rare → Valuable → Identity-confirming**

Difficulty is treated as evidence of worth. When something is hard to achieve, it becomes scarce. Scarcity drives social value. Social value makes achieving the thing a test of personal adequacy. At that point, the goal has fused with identity — it is no longer merely something you want, but something you need, because failing at it would constitute evidence about what kind of person you are.

Once that fusion happens, the stakes of any individual attempt inflate beyond the actual consequence of success or failure. The psychological cost of failure stops being proportional to what was lost and starts being proportional to what the failure seems to say about you. This is why highly intelligent, capable people can become strangely ineffective at things that matter to them — the situation that should call for clearer thinking is instead producing threat responses that cloud it.

There is also a reinforcement pattern embedded in scarcity experience that runs independently of the goal-identity fusion. When things are withheld — by genuine scarcity, parental gatekeeping, or arbitrary systems of difficulty — a consistent association forms: effort and sacrifice signal importance; ease signals suspicion. This isn’t irrational in the environments where it forms. Scarce things often were more valuable. But the heuristic generalizes beyond its validity and produces a bias toward unnecessary complexity, self-imposed friction, and discomfort with anything that comes without struggle. People will make their paths harder than they need to be not because difficulty serves the goal but because ease doesn’t feel like it counts.

This is a learned feature of the value function, not a character trait. But it runs invisibly, so it presents itself as character.



## Part IV: Narrative, Emotion, and the Motivation Chain

The sequence is worth being precise about, because getting it wrong produces the wrong interventions.

The actual chain is: **narrative → emotion → motivation → action**. Not: goal → rational assessment → action.

A goal only motivates to the degree that there is a story making it feel important. The story produces emotional weight. The emotional weight drives the orientation toward action. Reason enters the process downstream, largely in the role of justifying what emotion has already disposed you toward. This is not a controversial claim about human cognition — it is the observable structure of how motivation actually functions, visible in the fact that you can know, analytically, that something is in your interest without feeling any pull toward it at all.

This means that asking “what do I actually value?” through introspection alone has a fundamental limitation. Introspection gives you access to what you currently feel drawn toward, which is a downstream output of narrative structures that may not be directly visible. You feel the pull; you do not automatically see what is generating it.

The more diagnostic question is: what story am I running, where did it come from, and does the value it generates survive contact with reality?

When goals become too narratively loaded — when they become what I’ll call sacred — the system breaks down in characteristic ways. Sacred goals are ones where the narrative has inflated importance to the point that failure would be existential rather than merely costly. Exploration narrows because exploration risks failure, and failure at a sacred goal is categorically different from ordinary failure. The person becomes devoted in a way that looks like commitment from the outside but functions as self-erasure from the inside — the goal is not serving them, they are serving the goal, because the goal has become constitutive of who they are.

The key distinction is: important goals and sacred goals are not the same thing, and treating important goals as sacred is a consistent source of dysfunction that has nothing to do with how much the goal actually matters.


## Part V: The Devaluation Defense

So far the failure modes described have been passive — values absorbed without scrutiny, identity weight accumulated without awareness. But there is a more active pathology worth examining, because it reveals something the passive account misses: people don’t just inherit value functions, they also manipulate their own valuations as a performance strategy. And they rarely know they’re doing it.

Consider the person who navigates romantic pursuit through cultivated detachment. They are genuinely invested in securing a partner, yet they treat the whole enterprise with mild, ironic indifference. Paradoxically, this makes them effective. On the surface the mechanism is contradictory — why invest effort in something you claim not to value? — but it resolves cleanly when mapped onto the architecture.

The standard dysfunction runs like this: **high narrative value → identity fusion → threat response → performance degradation**. When someone wants something intensely, failure becomes a test of fundamental adequacy. These stakes recruit the wrong systems. Anxiety over outcome degrades the execution — ease, confidence, presence — that would have produced the outcome.

The devaluation defense short-circuits this at the first step. Artificially suppress the target’s perceived value, and you neutralize the identity threat that was impairing performance. **Low narrative value → no identity fusion → no threat response → cleaner execution.** The emotional conditions of not-caring have been manufactured without the actual reality of not-wanting. The desire is still entirely intact. The devaluation is a narrative shell constructed over the desire to make it less dangerous to carry.

Crucially, this rarely happens through deliberate reasoning. The person does not map out the psychological variables and consciously design a performance strategy. The visceral fear of an identity-threat searches for an escape route, and the psyche stumbles into devaluation as the path of least resistance. The system optimizes itself in the dark.

Which is why the same structure appears everywhere, usually unrecognized as the same structure. The person who has been working on a project for months and describes it to people they respect as “just something I’ve been messing around with” — not from modesty but from the need to pre-install a story where failure won’t count. The person who has been preparing obsessively for a high-stakes conversation and tells themselves, in the hours before, that the outcome doesn’t really matter to them, that they could walk away, that they’ve been through worse — constructing a temporary narrative of reduced stakes to get through the door without their hands shaking. The person who genuinely loves what they’re building but has learned to speak about it in the flat, hedged register of someone who could take it or leave it, because somewhere along the way they discovered that genuine enthusiasm invited a kind of scrutiny that felt dangerous.

In each case the formula is identical: want the thing, feel the threat of that wanting, construct a narrative of reduced value to execute without identity at stake. The want and the devaluation coexist because they serve different functions — the want drives the behavior, the devaluation manages the emotional cost of being visible while doing it.

The defense works, narrowly. But it carries a cost that the romantic pursuit case makes unusually clear.

You cannot suppress the narrative value of something for performance purposes and leave your underlying psychology intact. The person who systematically devalues intimacy to navigate dating without anxiety simultaneously dismantles their capacity to receive anything meaningful once they have what they pursued. The defense optimizes perfectly for acquisition and destroys the conditions for what comes after it. They get what they implicitly wanted and find they can’t value it now that they have it — because valuing it was the part they systematically eliminated to win the game.

The general version: a value function corrupted to manage performance anxiety can no longer give you accurate information about what things are worth. You have traded signal quality for emotional comfort. Execution becomes cleaner; the outcome becomes hollow.

This is worth naming because it is so frequently mistaken for emotional maturity. The person running the devaluation defense appears unbothered, effective, not needy — the thing contemporary culture most rewards the performance of. What they have actually built is a more elaborate version of the original problem: a value function untethered from reality, through active manipulation rather than passive inheritance. The authorship is real. The direction is still wrong.



## Part VI: What a Better Architecture Looks Like

The required shift is not from investment to detachment. That framing — care less, want less, lower your attachment — is the wrong diagnosis producing the wrong prescription.

Goals are cognitively necessary. They reduce the space of possible actions to a tractable subset, define what counts as progress, and generate the directional salience without which nothing feels worth doing. The depressive experience of anhedonia — where nothing seems worth pursuing — is what a motivational system looks like without a functioning goal structure. It is not peaceful. It is disorienting and corrosive.

The required shift is in what the goal is doing in the value function.

In the dysfunctional architecture, the goal is doing identity work. It is confirming or threatening the person’s sense of fundamental adequacy. The effort required is not effort toward an outcome — it is self-protection. In this frame, failure is damage rather than information.

In the functional architecture, the goal is doing directional work. It orients attention, generates structure, creates a gradient to move along. It matters, genuinely, without being constitutive of self. Failure in this frame is costly in proportion to what was actually lost — no more, no less.

The practical difference between these two architectures is large. In the first, the emotional system is recruited to defend the goal rather than execute toward it. In the second, the emotional system can be recruited for execution because there is nothing existential to defend.

Getting from the first to the second is not a matter of caring less. It is a matter of disaggregating the goal from the identity it has been made to carry. This requires, first, being able to see that the goal is carrying identity weight — which means tracing the narrative that generated its importance and asking whether that narrative survives scrutiny.



## Part VII: The Recalibration Problem

Claiming that the value function can be updated is the easy part. The harder problem — the one the essay has been circling without naming directly — is that the instrument you use to examine your value function is the value function itself.

This is not a minor complication. It means that what feels like scrutiny is often just the existing narrative examining itself and finding itself justified. The person who inherited a value system that prizes credentials will, when they introspect about whether credentials matter, tend to retrieve evidence that credentials matter — because the value function shapes what counts as evidence, what feels salient, and what conclusions feel rigorous versus self-serving. The system is not broken. It is working exactly as designed. It is just designed around the wrong inputs.

This is why the standard advice — “examine your values,” “question your assumptions” — is mostly useless without a more specific account of how to examine them in a way that isn’t simply the existing narrative examining itself. There are three operations that actually create distance from the value function rather than just running it more consciously.

The first is archaeological rather than introspective. Instead of asking “why do I want this,” which retrieves the narrative, ask “who first treated this as important, and what was at stake for them in my wanting it?” The goal is not to find the answer but to discover that there is a specific answer — a person, a context, a moment when a particular value got installed. Most inherited values feel like facts about the world precisely because their origin is invisible. Making the origin visible converts the value from a fact into a claim, which is the minimum condition for being able to evaluate it.

Take the compulsion toward credentials. Most people who feel it will, if they trace carefully enough, find it originates not in their own assessment of what credentials produce but in someone specific — a parent for whom credentials meant security, a teacher for whom they meant worthiness, a peer group for whom they meant belonging. The credential was a solution to their problem. It arrived feeling like a universal truth. Seeing that chain doesn’t dissolve the value, but it changes its status from given to proposed.

The second operation is testing expected value against direct experience before identity stakes accumulate. Narratives about things scale faster than experience of them — this is structural, not accidental, because the people describing things are almost always invested in your finding them valuable. The gap between how something is described by people inside it and how it actually feels to do it is nearly always larger than anticipated. The only way to close the gap is early engagement at low stakes, treated diagnostically — not as preparation for the real thing but as genuine data collection about whether the real thing is what the narrative says it is.

This is consistently avoided because the data might not support the conclusion. And the deeper problem is timing: by the time most people get close enough to a high-stakes goal to test it directly, the identity investment has already made clean observation nearly impossible. The value function will interpret ambiguous evidence as confirmation. This is why early, low-stakes, genuinely exploratory contact with the thing — before the goal becomes sacred — is the only window where the observation is relatively uncontaminated.

The third is coherence testing across the whole goal structure simultaneously. Individual goals can feel important in isolation while being systematically incoherent with each other — pursuing one undermines another, gains in one direction get partially cancelled by costs the narrow frame couldn’t see. The exhaustion this produces is different from the tiredness of working hard. It is the tiredness of movement that doesn’t accumulate, of perpetually catching up to where you already were. Coherence testing requires resisting the goal-pursuit instinct to narrow attention onto the current objective, and instead holding the whole structure visible long enough to ask whether its parts are reinforcing or eroding each other over time.

None of these operations resolves the self-reference problem entirely. The value function is always partly examining itself. But each operation creates a specific kind of friction against the narrative — a source of information that the narrative didn’t generate and therefore can’t fully absorb. That friction is what recalibration actually feels like from the inside: not clarity, but a growing difficulty in maintaining the story unchanged.

## Part VIII: What Quantitative Assessment Misses

There is a reason IQ scores don’t predict Nobel prizes.

IQ measures something real — processing speed, working memory, pattern recognition across abstract domains. The correlation with general life outcomes is genuine and reasonably robust. But the correlation with exceptional creative or intellectual output is surprisingly weak, and the reason is not measurement error. It is that the wrong thing is being measured.

Exceptional output — the kind that produces genuinely new knowledge, art, or understanding — does not primarily require high processing capacity. It requires a well-calibrated value function. Specifically: the ability to identify which problems are actually worth solving rather than which problems are legible and reward-adjacent; the disposition to pursue a question past the point where social proof runs out; the coherence to sustain a direction across years without the constant reinforcement of visible progress.

These are qualities of the goal-generation system, not the execution system. Someone with a corrupted value function — calibrated on social consensus, running inherited scripts, optimizing for proxies rather than the underlying variables — can have exceptional raw capacity and produce ordinary work, because their capacity is aimed at the wrong targets. Someone with a finely calibrated value function and moderate raw capacity can produce work that matters, because their effort is directed at things that are actually worth doing rather than things that are legible as worth doing.

This is what qualitative assessment tracks that quantitative assessment cannot: not how fast the engine runs, but where it is pointed. And pointing requires a value function that has been built through direct experience, examined for inherited distortions, and tested for internal coherence — not just assembled from whatever social consensus made available.

The practical implication is that the question “how capable am I?” is almost always less important than the question “how well-calibrated is my sense of what is worth doing?” Capability operates within the space the value function defines. A well-calibrated value function defines a space where capability actually compounds. A corrupted one defines a space where capability is perpetually busy without accumulating into anything that matters.



## Part IX: Intelligence, Evolving Goals, and a Better Definition of Success

The RL analogy, pushed one step further, reveals something the original framing obscures.

An AI system has a fixed objective function. The value function is a computational mechanism for pursuing a stable, externally specified goal. The agent is rational to the degree that it efficiently does what the objective requires. This works because the objective doesn’t change.

Human goals change. They evolve through experience, revision, and the encounter with new information about what things are actually like versus what they were supposed to be like. The objective function is not fixed. Which means that what counts as “intelligent” behavior in human life is not just efficient pursuit of current goals — it includes the capacity to update the goals themselves when updating is warranted.

This is the capability the conventional definition of success fails to account for. Success-as-achievement assumes a fixed target: you set a goal, you reach it, you succeed. This definition is coherent for any individual goal in isolation. It fails across time because reaching a goal reveals the next goal. The sense of completion is episodic and brief; the condition of not-yet-having-achieved is continuous. Optimizing for achievement in this sense is optimizing for a feeling that by construction cannot persist.

A more structurally sound account: success is the ongoing capacity to generate goals that are genuinely yours — calibrated through direct experience, coherent with each other, oriented toward expansion rather than mere accumulation — and to update them without losing continuity when experience reveals that revision is warranted.

This is demanding in a way that achievement is not, because it requires holding the goal structure itself open to revision while still committing enough to actually execute. The failure mode in one direction is rigidity — pursuing goals long after experience has indicated they were wrong, because updating feels like self-betrayal. The failure mode in the other direction is instability — revising goals so readily that no direction accumulates into anything. Both failures are common. The functional position is between them, and maintaining it is a skill that requires practice in the same way any other skill does.



## Conclusion: Authorship as Practice

Every person is running a value function. The question is not whether you have one — goallessness is not a stable psychological state; prolonged directionlessness is experienced as corrosive rather than peaceful. The question is whether the function you are running was designed or merely assembled from whatever inputs were most available.

Most people are running largely inherited code, calibrated on social consensus, reinforced by scarcity heuristics, carrying identity weight that inflates certain goals to sacred status. And some are running a more sophisticated version of the same problem — actively manipulating their own valuations to manage performance anxiety, mistaking the maneuver for emotional maturity. In both cases the value function is untethered from reality. In both cases the effort is real and the signal is corrupted.

The recalibration is not clean. The instrument you use to examine your value function is the value function itself, which means scrutiny is always partly compromised. What is possible is not perfect clarity but increasing friction against the inherited narrative — sources of information the narrative didn’t generate and therefore cannot fully absorb. Direct experience is the main source. It is also the one most consistently avoided, because it might not confirm what the narrative requires.

What this produces, when it works, is not a person with better answers but a person with better aim. Not the elimination of ambition but the redirection of it — toward problems that are actually worth solving rather than problems that are legible as worth solving, toward goals that cohere and compound rather than goals that merely signal. Capability is not the scarce resource. Well-calibrated direction is.

Most people spend their lives optimizing pursuit of goals they never examined, toward outcomes they were told to want, using a value function assembled from other people’s priorities. The work described here is not therapy, not self-help, not the elimination of ambition. It is something more basic: deciding that the equipment you use to navigate your own life should actually belong to you. Once you see the function clearly — where it came from, what it is actually tracking, whether its outputs reflect anything real — you cannot unsee it. That visibility is the beginning of something. Not ease. But direction that is genuinely yours.
